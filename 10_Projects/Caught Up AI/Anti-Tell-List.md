# Caught Up AI - Anti-Tell Do/Don't List (D4)

The hard current blocklist for every full AI-generated piece. This is the mechanical gate: it is cheap to run, high-value, and you re-check it on every piece before anything else. It absorbs the v1 blocklist from [[Writing-Manual]] section 7 and extends it to the live 2025-2026 signature, which has moved off the old vocabulary and onto significance-inflation and copula-avoidance.

How to use this file:
- The lexical, syntactic, structural, and discourse sections below are DON'T lists. Each banned item names the legitimate craft move it counterfeits and how to do the real thing. That craft-vs-counterfeit pairing is the whole point: you are not banning a device, you are banning the fake version of it.
- The table at the end is the fast reference: device, real move, counterfeit, keep-it-real test.
- Register variety across a batch is the other half of the defense. This file kills the per-piece tell; cross-piece sameness is governed by [[Cross-Piece-Sameness-Rubric]] and the register palette in [[Register-Specs]].

Two permanent rules that override any corpus evidence, any model update, and any argument that "the source did it":
- NO em-dashes anywhere. Not the long dash, not an en-dash standing in for one. Use commas, colons, periods, or parentheses. This is Samuel's voice standard and the single loudest journalist-facing AI flag.
- NO emojis anywhere.

One framing to carry through everything below: the old AI vocabulary (delve, tapestry, robust) is burned and newer models already suppress it. Avoiding it is table stakes, not safety. The active signature is now the habit of TELLING the reader that something matters instead of showing it, and the habit of dodging the plain verb "is." Hunt those two first.

## 1. Lexical (banned words and phrases)

### Family A: legacy vocabulary (still banned, now table stakes)
- delve, tapestry, testament, underscore, pivotal, crucial, vital, robust, nuanced, multifaceted, holistic, paradigm, myriad, plethora, foster, leverage (verb), resonate, navigate (figurative), landscape (figurative), realm, beacon, embark, journey (figurative), seamless, ever-evolving, fast-paced, game-changer.
- Why it is no longer enough: "delve" spiked in academic abstracts after late 2023 then dropped sharply through 2025 as writers learned to strip it. Newer models self-suppress this family. A piece clean of Family A is not human-passing, it has just cleared the floor.
- You have drifted if: you feel relieved after scanning for these words and stop there. The real risk is in Families C and D below.

### Family B: current single-word offenders (2025-2026, the live ones)
- showcase, boast / boasts, intricate / intricacies, interplay, meticulous / meticulously, garner, bolster / bolstered, enduring, vibrant, nestled, renowned, groundbreaking, harness, streamline, utilize, facilitate, enhance, exemplify, profound, comprehensive, innovative, cutting-edge, synergy, underpinnings, align / alignment.
- These are the words "normally rare" in plain reporting whose frequency spikes in machine text. "Utilize" where "use" works, "enhance" where "improve" works, "garner" where "get" or "win" works.
- You have drifted if: you reached for the longer Latinate synonym when a plain English word said it. Prefer use over utilize, get over garner, help over facilitate, build over harness.

### Family C: the significance-inflation lexicon (the strongest current tell)
- "stands as a testament to" / "serves as a testament to", "plays a vital / crucial / pivotal role", "underscores the importance of", "highlights the enduring legacy", "reflects a broader trend", "symbolizing its ongoing", "a powerful reminder of", "marking a turning point", "cementing its place".
- These attach cosmic weight to ordinary facts. They are the single most reliable machine signature in current nonfiction.
- This is the highest-value ban in the whole file. The reflex they share is asserting that a fact is important rather than showing the fact and letting the reader feel the weight. See the Significance row in the craft table.
- You have drifted if: a sentence tells the reader something is important, significant, vital, a testament, a turning point, or a reminder. Delete the assertion. Put the concrete fact that earns it. If no concrete fact exists, the significance was hollow, so cut the whole beat.

### Family D: the copula-avoidance verbs (ban the reflex)
- serves as, stands as, represents, marks, features, offers, boasts, comprises, where the honest verb is "is" or "has".
- Canonical example: "X is a major hub" rewritten as "X serves as a major railway hub." The plain copula was correct and the rewrite is the tell.
- You have drifted if: you wrote "serves as" / "stands as" / "represents" and "is" would have carried the sentence. Allow the plain copula. "Is" and "has" are not weak verbs to be avoided, they are the honest ones.

### Family E: faux-insight nouns and phrases
- "valuable insights", "new insights into", "the complex interplay", "a deeper understanding", "key takeaways", "a glimpse into", "a fascinating glimpse", "a rich tapestry of", "the broader implications".
- "providing valuable insights into" and "into the complex interplay" are thousands of times more common in machine text than in human text.
- You have drifted if: you promised the reader an insight, a glimpse, or a deeper understanding instead of delivering the specific thing. State the thing; let the reader judge whether it is an insight.

### Family F: hollow intensifier and adjective pairings
- breathtaking, stunning, remarkable, striking, compelling, fascinating, profound, rich, "diverse array", "wide range", "deeply" plus anything.
- These read as travel-brochure and press-release positivity. Current-events copy earns an adjective with a fact, it never leads with the adjective.
- You have drifted if: an adjective is doing the work a fact should do. "A remarkable turnout" is empty; "turnout doubled the 2022 figure" is the fact. Cut the adjective or attach the number.

### The transition-word reflex (sentence-head signposts)
- Banned at the head of a sentence: "Additionally,", "Moreover,", "Furthermore,", "Notably,", "Importantly,", "Crucially,", "Indeed,", "Ultimately,".
- Connective overuse is a measured machine marker. A human writer connects ideas through the sentence, not by bolting an adverbial signpost onto the front.
- You have drifted if: two or more sentences in the piece open on one of these. Carry the connection inside the sentence instead, or just let the next sentence follow.

### Elegant variation (the synonym-cycling tell)
- The model refuses to repeat a key noun, so it cycles synonyms: "the artists... the non-conformists... these creators... the visionaries", all meaning the same people.
- Real writers repeat the plain word. Forced synonym-cycling is a lexical fingerprint.
- You have drifted if: you renamed the subject three different ways to avoid saying it twice. Repeat the plain noun. Repetition of the right word is not a flaw.

## 2. Syntactic (banned sentence-level habits)

- Low burstiness. Sentence lengths cluster around one mean, often 15 to 22 words, with little variance. The AP Lang source corpus swings hard between a 40-word periodic sentence and a 4-word declarative. You have drifted if: no sentence in the piece is under 8 words and none is over 30, or adjacent sentences keep landing at the same length. Fix by mandating at least one very short sentence and at least one long complex one per piece, and never letting three sentences in a row match in length. (Detail in [[Writing-Manual]] section 4.)
- Monotone clause architecture even when length varies. The deeper tell beneath burstiness: every sentence is Subject-Verb-Object opening on the grammatical subject, clauses bolted with one connector ("and", "which", "as"). You have drifted if: nearly every sentence starts on its subject. Vary the grammar, not just the count: open on a dependent clause, an adverbial, or a prepositional phrase; alternate periodic and loose; drop one earned fragment.
- Present-participle significance tails. A factual main clause followed by an "-ing" phrase that editorializes its importance: "The bridge opened in 1932, connecting two communities and contributing to regional growth." The trailing participle claiming broader meaning is a hallmark machine move. You have drifted if: a sentence ends on a comma plus an "-ing" phrase that asserts why the fact matters. Cut the coda or make it a fact, not a claim of significance.
- Stacked participial or absolute openers. "Having considered the evidence, recognizing the stakes, and understanding the context, the city..." reads as machine throat-clearing. At most one such opener per piece, and only when it carries real subordination.
- Mechanical parallelism and over-symmetry. Clauses balanced to the syllable, lists where every member has identical grammatical shape, paragraph after paragraph on the same template. Real parallelism is occasional and load-bearing; machine parallelism is the default cadence. You have drifted if: the symmetry is the background hum of the whole piece rather than a chosen effect at one moment. See the Parallelism row in the craft table.
- Tricolon on autopilot. Three-item lists everywhere: "adjective, adjective, and adjective." Not every series is three. You have drifted if: most of the lists in the piece are threes. Use two, use four, use an asymmetric list whose members differ in length and shape. See the Tricolon row in the craft table.
- Negative-parallelism templates as syntax. "not just X, but Y", "it's not X, it's Y", "not only... but also". The shape itself, repeated, is the tell even when one instance passes. You have drifted if: this construction appears more than once, or it appears at all without a real opposition behind it. See the Antithesis row in the craft table.
- Uniform paragraph mass. The model produces paragraphs of suspiciously even weight, often three to four sentences each. Human argument is ragged: a one-sentence paragraph next to a seven-sentence one. You have drifted if: every paragraph in the piece is roughly the same length. Make the paragraph lengths uneven on purpose.

## 3. Structural (banned piece-level shapes)

- The tidy three-point bow. A conclusion that restates the body's three points and ties them with a uniform ribbon ("In conclusion", "In summary", "Ultimately, X is a testament to Y"). The corpus closes on a turn, a button, or a call to action, never on a recap. You have drifted if: the last paragraph summarizes what the reader just read.
- The "Challenges and Future Directions" close. "Despite its [positive], [subject] faces challenges, but with continued [effort], the future looks [adjective]." A canonical machine ending. Ban speculative both-handed forward-looking codas.
- Cosmic-frame openers. "In a world where...", "In today's world", "In an age of...", "In the ever-evolving [X]". ("In the ever-evolving" is measured thousands of times more common in machine text.) Open on the specific situation or fact, not a cosmic frame.
- Throat-clearing rhetorical-question opener. "What does it mean to be [X]? Have you ever wondered...?" The corpus reserves the rhetorical question for a mid-piece pivot. One pointed question per piece, placed at a turn, never as a warm-up. See the Rhetorical question row in the craft table.
- Pseudo-conversational pivots. "Here's the thing", "Here's the kicker", "But here's the catch", "The catch?" Manufactured intimacy. Cut entirely.
- Move-announcing filler. "Let's dive in", "Let's unpack", "Let's explore", "At the end of the day", "When all is said and done". Frames that announce a move instead of making it. Make the move.
- Sweeping range gestures. "from the boardroom to the classroom", "from Wall Street to Main Street". A false-comprehensiveness flourish. Name the actual specific instead.
- The significance-coda paragraph. A final beat that zooms out to claim the subject "reflects a broader trend" or "reminds us" of something universal. Current-events copy lands on the concrete stakes, not a homily. (This is Family C operating at the paragraph level.)
- Inline-header vertical lists and title-case section headings in running prose. Bolded "Header: explanation" lines signal listicle scaffolding under the text. Foreign to op-ed and essay register. The piece is continuous prose.
- Symmetrical section architecture. An intro that previews three points, three equal body sections, then a recap. The skeleton is too visible. The corpus arc (open, build, close on a single engine) is asymmetric by design. See [[Writing-Manual]] section 3.

## 4. Discourse (banned stances and rhetorical postures)

- Vague authority. "studies show", "experts agree", "research suggests", "observers have noted", "critics argue", "industry reports indicate", with nothing attached. For this product this doubles as an ACCURACY hazard: an unattributed claim is both an AI tell and an unverifiable fact. HARD RULE: every factual claim names a real, checkable source or it is cut. This single rule serves the anti-tell goal and the accuracy gate at once.
- Implied many sources from one or two. "multiple outlets", "widespread coverage", "commentators across the spectrum". Name the actual outlets or drop the claim.
- Over-hedging. Stacked qualifiers ("it could be argued that", "in many cases", "to some extent", "arguably", "generally speaking") that drain the piece of a position. The corpus takes ONE clear stance. Hedge only where the fact is genuinely uncertain, and then attribute the uncertainty.
- False balance. "on one hand... on the other hand" that gives symmetric weight to asymmetric positions and commits to nothing. Distinct from genuine concession, which grants ground then turns. The machine both-sides and stops; the craft move concedes and advances. See the Concession row in the craft table.
- Significance-inflation as a stance. Narrating why an ordinary fact "matters", "is important", or "highlights a broader truth" instead of showing it. Show the stakes through the fact; never assert that something is significant.
- Promotional positivity. Press-release and travel-brochure warmth: "a vibrant community", "a commitment to excellence", "breathtaking". Machine text skews positive and smooth. A current-events catch-up is neutral to pointed, never boosterish.
- Empty metadiscourse. "It's important to note that", "It is worth mentioning", "It should be noted". ("important to note" runs thousands of times more common in machine text.) Cut the frame; state the thing.
- Over-explaining the obvious. Spelling out what an informed reader already knows, restating the premise before advancing. The machine pads to hit length and to seem thorough. Trust the reader; cut to the new information.
- Consensus voice with no idiolect. No specific opinions, no friction, no unexpected word choice, no point of view that could only be one writer's. Reads as the consensus of the internet. The fix is a real authorial vantage, the thing every corpus passage has.

## 5. Craft vs counterfeit (the fast reference table)

Each row pairs the real device with its machine fake and the one test that tells them apart. When in doubt, run the test in the last column.

| Device | Real move | AI counterfeit | Keep-it-real test |
|---|---|---|---|
| Antithesis | Two genuinely opposed ideas held across the piece to drive the argument (Kennedy: ordinary citizens vs a tiny handful of executives; Saujani: perfection vs bravery). Deleting it collapses the point. | The "it's not just X, it's Y" / "not merely X, but Y" / "not only... but also" sing-song, where X and Y are not opposed, just dressed as contrast to manufacture depth. | Are X and Y actually in tension, and does the piece depend on it? If you can delete the construction and lose no argument, it was a counterfeit. Ban the fixed template; require any antithesis to be paraphrasable as a real claim ("the cost falls on A while the benefit goes to B"). |
| Anaphora / repetition | One repeated frame per piece that builds cumulative force (Hazlitt's "it is to..."; Albright's escalating refrain). Each repetition adds a new, escalating instance. | Repetition for cadence with no escalation: "We must act. We must lead. We must inspire." with no rising specificity. | Does each repeated unit add a NEW, escalating, concrete instance? If the repeats are interchangeable in order, it is autopilot. Cap at one anaphoric frame per piece; the final instance must be the heaviest. |
| Tricolon / rule of three | A three-part list used ONCE, members genuinely distinct, the third landing hardest (climactic order). | Triads everywhere as the default list length, members often near-synonyms padding to three. | Count the lists. If most are threes, vary them: use two or four, make members differ in length and shape. One real tricolon per piece, ordered so the last is strongest. |
| Rhetorical question | A single pointed question at a PIVOT, turning the argument or implicating the reader, answered by what follows. | Throat-clearing opener questions ("What does it really mean to...? Have you ever wondered...?") that perform curiosity without advancing anything. | Is the question at a turn, and does the next sentence answer or weaponize it? If it is the first sentence or merely introduces a topic, cut it. One per piece, at the pivot, never at the open. |
| Concession | Grant the opposing side something REAL, then turn against it (Sanders, Luce). The concession costs the writer something and makes the rebuttal land. | False balance: "on one hand... on the other hand" that grants both sides and commits to neither, ending in symmetric mush. | After the concession, does the piece TURN and take a side? A concession not followed by a rebuttal is both-sidesing. Require concede-then-advance; ban concede-then-balance. |
| Significance / stakes | SHOW why something matters through a concrete particular, scene, or number (Kelley's child laborers; pathos on one specific detail). Significance is demonstrated, never asserted. | The significance-inflation lexicon: "stands as a testament to", "underscores the importance of", "a powerful reminder of". Importance asserted as a verbal tic on ordinary facts. | Search the draft for any sentence that TELLS the reader something is important, significant, vital, or a testament. Delete the assertion; replace with the concrete fact that earns it. If no fact exists, cut the beat. |
| Short declarative | A short sentence landing a concrete turn or button, set against a long accumulating sentence (Barry: "Certainty creates strength."). It carries weight. | The fake-profound one-line paragraph stating an abstraction ("Change is hard." "Time will tell.") for pseudo-gravity; or uniform short sentences flattening rhythm. | Does the short sentence carry a concrete particular or a real turn, and does it cut against a genuinely long neighbor? An abstract one-liner floating as its own paragraph is the counterfeit. |
| Parallelism / balanced syntax | Periodic, balanced syntax at a climactic moment for emphasis (Thatcher's "Others... He..."), deployed against a mostly varied background. | Relentless symmetry: every clause balanced, every paragraph templated, syntax monotone even when length varies. The default cadence, not a chosen effect. | Is the balanced sentence an exception against ragged prose, or is the whole piece balanced? Require deliberate raggedness; reserve full symmetry for one or two emphatic moments. |

## 6. The mechanical pass (run this on every piece)

Fast, cheap, do it every time, before any judgment call about quality:
1. Search for em-dashes and emojis. Any hit is an automatic fail. Remove and re-scan.
2. Search Family A through F words and the significance-inflation phrases. Each hit: justify it against the register or cut it.
3. Search sentence heads for "Additionally / Moreover / Furthermore / Notably / Importantly / Crucially / Indeed / Ultimately". More than one in the piece: rework.
4. Find every sentence that asserts something is important, significant, vital, a testament, a turning point, or a reminder. Replace each with the concrete fact, or cut the beat. This is the highest-yield step.
5. Find every "serves as / stands as / represents / marks / features / boasts" and test whether "is" or "has" is the honest verb. If yes, use it.
6. Check burstiness: at least one sentence under 8 words and at least one over 30; no three same-length sentences in a row.
7. Check every factual claim names a real checkable source. Unattributed claim: cut it. This is both the AI-tell gate and the accuracy gate.
8. Check the close: it lands on a turn, button, or concrete stake, not a recap or a forward-looking homily.

A piece that fails any of steps 1, 4, or 7 does not get a quality read. It goes back first.

## Sourcing note

Lexical findings are evidence-backed: the "delve" spike-then-decline in academic abstracts (Liang and Zou, Nature Human Behaviour 2025; arXiv 2502.09606); the Stanford/Zou million-paper study naming "pivotal", "intricate", and "showcase" as genre-rare words whose frequency rose; arXiv 2506.21817 on moreover/furthermore connective overuse and longer uniform sentences; the Wikipedia "Signs of AI writing" catalogue (copula-avoidance, significance-inflation, present-participle tails, vague attribution, the "Challenges and Future Directions" close); Pangram Labs phrase-frequency multipliers ("serves as a testament" about 4,000x, "into the complex interplay" about 21,000x, "In the ever-evolving" about 11,000x, "important to note" about 3,000x). Burstiness and perplexity material is from detection-vendor sources and is directional, not authoritative. Do not tune to one detector; tune toward the human distribution and validate with a blind human read against real professional nonfiction of the same register.
