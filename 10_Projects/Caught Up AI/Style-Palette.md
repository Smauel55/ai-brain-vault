# Style Palette

The seven-register backbone for [[Caught Up AI]]. This is the document that beats cross-piece sameness. Every piece is written in exactly one register; rotating registers across a weekly batch is the primary defense against a batch reading like one voice with the topic swapped out.

Companion documents: [[Register-Specs]] (the measurable bands and drift cues for each register), [[Structural-Templates]] (the skeletons), [[Annotated-Exemplars]] (worked examples), [[Generation-Briefs]] (the per-piece instructions), and [[Writing-Manual]] (the master rules every register inherits). For where the facts come from, see [[Content-Sourcing]].

## How to use this palette

- Pick one register per piece, before drafting. The register decides the power-relation, the sentence rhythm, and the closing move. Do not blend two in one piece.
- Rotate across the batch. A normal week mixes the sober anchor (The Ledger) with hotter and more intimate registers so no two pieces share a skeleton. A good weekly spread looks like: one Ledger, plus a selection from an Open Letter, a Reckoning, a Tribute, a Long Think, a Witness Stand, and a Long Look, never the same two twice running.
- Match register to the news, not the news to the register. If the story has no nameable opposing party, it is not a Reckoning. If it is an occasion to honor rather than argue, it is a Tribute. If it has no genuine awe in it, it is not a Long Look. Forcing the wrong register is itself a tell.
- Emulate craft only. We borrow rhetorical architecture, sentence craft, and register from the exemplar passages. We never borrow their topic, their period diction, or the pretense of being a historical author, and we never reproduce, summarize, or paraphrase any source prose. Facts only from sources; the prose is original.
- Two hard personal rules sit above everything: no em-dashes anywhere (commas, semicolons, colons, periods, and parentheses carry every suspension and turn) and no emojis. These are not stylistic preferences; they are gates.
- Two editorial gates also sit above register choice (see [[Editorial-Standards]], Gate 0). NEUTRALITY: no piece takes a political side, attacks a specific person, or attacks a group; civic topics are fine, partisan stance is not, on any tier. This constrains routing: The Open Letter's addressee and The Reckoning's culprit may never be a politician, a party, or an identifiable political figure (route those stories to The Ledger or drop them). PLAIN KNOWLEDGE: every unexplained term must be common knowledge to a high schooler; specialist terms get a plain-language gloss on first use or are cut.

## Evergreen vs event-pegged registers (a routing lens, not a rule)

The seven registers split on whether they need a live news event or sit naturally on a durable, evergreen subject. This is a tendency to route by, not a restriction: an evergreen subject can still be pegged to recent news, and a current event can be handled in an evergreen-leaning voice. Use it to spread a batch, not to forbid a pairing.

- Need a live event or trigger: The Reckoning (hard-requires a nameable culprit plus a datable trigger), The Open Letter (needs a named addressee and a precipitating occasion). The Ledger leans current and policy-driven but flexes evergreen when a stable subject is framed as a live question.
- Built for evergreen or durable subjects: The Long Look (defined against breaking-news urgency; wants an awe-worthy system or process), The Long Think (turns over the durable human theme behind the news), The Tribute (an occasion to honor, which can be an idea or institution, not only an event), The Witness Stand (runs on shared human experience, with the news as the doorway).

Why it matters: evergreen pieces are not a fallback. They are the native habitat for roughly four of the seven voices, and they are well-attested in real AP Lang source material, where timeless essays sit alongside event-driven argument. A week that mixes current and evergreen reads more like the actual AP corpus than an all-event week would. It also tends to ease sourcing, since the evergreen-suited registers can draw on public-domain and durable material rather than the thin current-source pool, though that is a side benefit to plan around, never a reason to force a register onto the wrong story.

## Why seven, and how the proposals were reconciled

The partition principle: a register earns a slot only if it differs from every other on BOTH the power-relation it sets up (who speaks to whom, from what authority) AND its sentence-shape distribution (does it suspend, pile, chop, balance, or meander). Those are the two dimensions the ear catches first, and the two that default AI prose flattens hardest. Cosmetic-only differences were merged. Differences that survived both tests were kept. The result is seven registers, the top of the 6-7 target. Current-events work needs both a sober daily workhorse and enough hot, intimate, and ceremonial range that a weekly batch never repeats a skeleton.

How the competing proposals were resolved:

- The two periodic registers were SPLIT. Suspended periodic syntax forks on vector. Speaking up to a more-powerful addressee whose own principles are turned back on them (the open letter) is audibly not the same machine as pressing a gathered crowd toward indignation and action (the charge). Same architecture, opposite target, different rhythm at the close. Kept as The Open Letter and The Reckoning.
- Analytic argument and institutional exposition were MERGED. On a 350 to 600 word piece, the agentless agency-report voice and the claim-qualify-counter op-ed run the same low-device, attribution-bearing, even-cadence prose where authority is the evidence rather than the self. Collapsing them yields the single most-used register and prevents two near-identical voices sitting in one batch. Kept as The Ledger.
- The familiar essay and the lived-identity essay were KEPT SEPARATE. They are audibly distinct on the source of ethos (the observer's candor versus the credential of having lived it) and on person (sideways to a peer versus the self offered outward). Kept as The Long Think and The Witness Stand.
- Lyric awe and descriptive witness were KEPT as their own register and not folded into exposition. Rendering a phenomenon to produce wonder rather than to argue a position is a different job and a different rhythm. Kept as The Long Look.
- Ceremonial and commemorative was KEPT rather than dissolved into oratory. Epideictic consecration recedes behind its subject and closes on quiet elevation, the exact opposite vector from the charge to act. Kept as The Tribute.
- Satire and irony were FOLDED, not minted as a seventh voice. A standalone satire register is a standing liability for a product whose accuracy is non-negotiable, and the corpus support is thin. Its deadpan-ventriloquism craft is annexed to The Long Think as a tonal mode.

Why this beats both failure modes. Per-piece AI tell: each register carries its own sentence-length distribution and cadence rather than the flat mid-length uniformity of default model prose, so committing fully to one register's syntax per piece is itself the defense. Cross-piece sameness: the seven span six genuinely different power-relations and five different rhythmic skeletons, so rotating them forces structurally different pieces, not synonym swaps. The historical anchors (roughly 1780 to 1939) are load-bearing precisely because they supply suspension, cumulative anaphora, and balanced antithesis that modern prose has flattened.

## The seven registers

### 1. The Ledger (anchor, default workhorse)

- Identity: an impersonal, evidence-forward voice where authority belongs to the documented record, not to any speaking self. It lays out and lightly weighs the facts rather than performing conviction.
- Lineage: Q1 synthesis prose. Government and agency reports, reference and policy exposition, business and data journalism, and the measured analytic op-ed. The 20th to 21st century explanatory and argumentative column.
- When to use it: the default for straight catch-up reporting and explainers. A primer on a law or program, a "what is happening and why" piece, or a take that weighs a contested question. Use whenever credibility depends on sounding sourced and disinterested and a visible authorial personality would undercut it. Pair it in a batch as the sober anchor against the hotter registers.
- Anchoring exemplars: U.S. Government Accountability Office, USPS financial-condition report (2011); James E. McWilliams, food-miles op-ed (2007); Ilya Somin, eminent-domain commentary (c. 2015); Anne Trubek, "Handwriting Just Doesn't Matter," New York Times (2016); Kirk Savage, Monument Wars (2009).
- Signature craft: lead by naming the situation, finding, or question flatly and impersonally; keep first person nearly absent. Attribute and quantify (named bodies, figures, dates). Low device density on purpose; the plainness is the ethos. Handle counterargument by acknowledgment then qualification ("yes, but the data show"). Order by logic, not drama: cause, scope, consequence, status. Even, controlled sentence lengths. Close on an assessment or implication, never a flourish. Commas set appositive definitions; colons introduce enumerations.
- How it differs: from The Long Look, it argues toward a position rather than rendering a phenomenon. From The Long Think, the self is absent. The routing test against The Long Think is the withheld turn: if the thesis arrives in the first sentence and is then supported, it is Ledger; if the piece reasons to an earned turn mid-stride, it is Long Think.

### 2. The Open Letter

- Identity: one voice addressing one more-powerful party, deference on the surface and moral pressure underneath, with the public invited to overhear.
- Lineage: 18th to 19th century epistolary suasion and the open letter to a patron or sovereign; the classical petition, extended into reasoned appeals to authority.
- When to use it: pieces framed as a direct address to a named power (a CEO, a regulator, an institution) when the move is to hold someone accountable by appealing to their own stated principles rather than attacking them. Best when the subject is delicate and the tone must read as measured, deferential, and morally serious.
- Anchoring exemplars: Benjamin Banneker, Letter to Thomas Jefferson (1791); Mohandas K. Gandhi, Letter to Lord Irwin (1930); Abigail Adams, Letter to John Quincy Adams (1780).
- Signature craft: long periodic sentences whose stacked subordination performs the deference and enacts the care of the thinking. Open on a concessive or subordinate clause and suspend the main verb; let a colon, not a dash, mark the turn into it. The signature move is to quote the addressee's own principles or words back at them so the pressure looks like their idea. Anaphoric tricolon to build weight. Concession and paradox used to disarm. Warmth where kinship exists. The whole piece pressures by making refusal feel like self-contradiction. Commas and semicolons carry the suspensions.
- How it differs: from The Reckoning, the power vector is inverted (the lower party petitioning up versus a speaker charging a room) and the addressee is a single named party rather than a diffuse "we." Hardening rule: keep a named second-person addressee throughout. If the piece addresses a diffuse "we" or "you all," it is a Reckoning.

### 3. The Reckoning

- Identity: a speaker with moral or institutional standing pressing a gathered audience toward feeling and action, turning a shared principle into an accusation the reader cannot dodge.
- Lineage: Progressive-era reform oratory and 20th century American civic and presidential address; classical deliberative and forensic speech to an assembly.
- When to use it: pieces that argue a public stake and want urgency, when the goal is to make the reader feel the scale of a wrong or a duty now and to charge them with it. Best for accountability angles with a clear public interest and a nameable opposing handful. Reserve for stories where the facts genuinely support attributing responsibility.
- Anchoring exemplars: Florence Kelley, NAWSA child-labor speech (1905); John F. Kennedy, opening statement on steel prices (1962); Cesar Chavez, article on nonviolence (1978); Madeleine Albright, Mount Holyoke commencement (1997).
- Signature craft: build a long suspended runway, usually anaphoric, that loads public context ("when we are confronted with, when we are asking"), then drop the main clause that names the offense and its scale; follow the enormous sentence with a short, hard consequence sentence. Antithesis pits the many against the few. A present-tense pivot puts the audience inside the scene now. Statistics fronted as moral fact. Escalating exemplification grows in scale. The close is a charge to act. The "when, when" clauses and semicolons carry the suspension.
- How it differs: from The Tribute, this is the highest near-merge risk in the palette, so the separation is hard-gated. The Reckoning MUST end on an imperative or charge and MUST carry a nameable culprit plus a datable trigger. If there is no nameable opposing party, the piece is not a Reckoning; route it to Tribute or Ledger. Irony is banned here (it lives in The Long Think).

### 4. The Tribute

- Identity: a dignified voice consecrating a person or moment, binding a single life or gesture to shared values. The speaker recedes behind the honored subject.
- Lineage: classical epideictic (praise) oratory; the modern eulogy, tribute, dedication, and commencement honor.
- When to use it: obituary-adjacent pieces, anniversaries, milestones, awards, and farewells. Any time the news is an occasion to honor or mark rather than to argue or report. The warm-formal counterpart to The Reckoning. Elegiac and ceremonial without slipping into argument or sentimentality.
- Anchoring exemplars: Barack Obama, Rosa Parks statue dedication (2013); Margaret Thatcher, eulogy for Ronald Reagan (2004); Claudia "Lady Bird" Johnson, tribute to Eleanor Roosevelt (1964).
- Signature craft: balanced, cadenced syntax meant to be spoken slowly, with antithesis of the small and the large ("slight in stature but mighty in courage"). She/others anaphora that sets the figure apart. Narrative into claim: a small concrete anecdote opened out into significance. Understatement preferred to hyperbole; grandeur comes from restraint. The speaker's authority is borrowed from the dignity of the subject. Closes on quiet elevation, the figure placed in a longer history, never on a call to action. Commas and colons carry the balance.
- How it differs: from The Reckoning, the vector is inverted (consecrate rather than accuse). Hardening rules that follow from the distinctness audit: the Tribute MUST close on a benediction or placement-in-history and is FORBIDDEN an imperative close; the fronted-statistic-as-moral-fact move (a Reckoning signature) is forbidden inside a Tribute; device density runs lower than The Reckoning. From The Witness Stand, the locus of the "I" inverts: first person appears only to register debt, affection, or shared grief, never to make the speaker the subject, and self-deprecation-as-ethos is forbidden (it is a Witness Stand signature and reads as a leak here).

### 5. The Long Think

- Identity: a writer thinking aloud to a peer-reader, exploratory and unhurried, granting the other side real ground before turning. No crowd to move and no power to flatter.
- Lineage: the 18th to 19th century familiar and periodical essay (Hazlitt) through the 20th century literary and argumentative magazine piece (Lippmann, Sanders, Louv). Annexes controlled satire and deadpan irony as a tonal mode (Wilson, The Onion).
- When to use it: pieces that take a considered position on a durable human theme behind the news (attention, money, place, work, language) and want a mind visibly turning the question over rather than rallying or honoring. Also the home for controlled irony that punctures hype or jargon without sacrificing accuracy.
- Anchoring exemplars: William Hazlitt, On the Want of Money (1827); Walter Lippmann, The Indispensable Opposition (1939); Scott Russell Sanders, Staying Put (1993); Edward O. Wilson, The Future of Life (satiric ventriloquism mode, 2002/2009); The Onion, MagnaSoles (satiric mode, 2005).
- Signature craft: cumulative loose sentences that discover their point mid-stride, often anaphoric ("it is to; it is to"). Concession then rebuttal: grant the opposing view fully, then turn on a withheld metaphor or distinction; reason forward from shared premises to an earned conclusion. Pathos earned by exact particulars and a tonal shift, not declaration. In the satiric mode, adopt the target's own register straight-faced so the excess indicts itself; the imitation does the critique. Closes inward, on a turn of thought. Semicolons separate major members; colons mark the pivot.
- How it differs: from The Witness Stand, by the absence of the writer's biography. The Long Think "I" is a reasoner, never a memoirist. A single first-person remembered scene starring the author is an automatic register violation here, not a stylistic note. From The Long Look, the gate is commitment: The Long Think commits to a position or reframes a question, while The Long Look withholds all moral judgment. The Long Think uses the short-flat-declarative snap more sparingly than The Long Look.

### 6. The Witness Stand

- Identity: a contemporary first-person voice building authority from lived experience offered up, turning a personal story into a public claim with self-aware warmth.
- Lineage: late-20th and 21st century memoir essay, the personal-identity address, and the literary commencement speech. Distinct from the older familiar essay by its anecdotal, confiding, self-deprecating ethos.
- When to use it: pieces that benefit from a human vantage on a shared experience (immigration, work and ambition, identity, belonging) when persuasion should run through a credible personal story rather than detached observation or institutional evidence.
- Anchoring exemplars: Reshma Saujani, essay in American Like Me (2018); Sonia Sotomayor, A Latina Judge's Voice (2001); Rita Dove, University of Virginia commencement (2016).
- Signature craft: conversational mid-length sentences with deliberate anecdote and scene from the writer's own life. Authority by vulnerability and qualification ("this one is different; this is personal") and by self-deprecation that disarms. Anaphoric refrain that confides rather than commands. Redefinition of an ordinary word (bravery, hunger, voice) as the organizing move. A driving antithesis drawn from experience. Vary sentence length freely, from fragments to looping middles, to mimic a live speaking mind. Closes by handing the lesson to the reader as a gift, not a directive. Parentheses, commas, and colons render the asides.
- How it differs: from The Long Think, by the presence of the writer's biography as the load-bearing evidence. Discriminator gate: whose life supplies the anecdotes? If the writer's own, Witness Stand; if a third party being honored, Tribute. From The Reckoning, intimacy over urgency.

### 7. The Long Look

- Identity: an absorbed observer rendering a phenomenon, place, or process with precision and awe, so the prose enacts the complexity it admires rather than arguing a position.
- Lineage: 19th century descriptive natural-history and travel writing through modern narrative nonfiction and popular science and history (Barry); the contemplative practitioner statement.
- When to use it: pieces that explain a complex system, place, or process (a market, a storm, a technology, an institution) and want the reader to feel its scale and intricacy. Use when the job is illumination and fascination, not persuasion. Reserve for news that is genuinely awe-worthy and avoid for conflict or accountability stories.
- Anchoring exemplars: John M. Barry, Rising Tide (2005); John M. Barry, The Great Influenza (2004); Maya Lin, Boundaries (2000); Richard Louv, Last Child in the Woods (2005).
- Signature craft: short flat declaratives snapped against long accumulating technical sentences ("Not only is it acted upon; it acts"). Accretive lists whose growing clauses mirror the subject's complexity. A controlling antithesis often frames the mystery (certainty versus uncertainty, order versus force). Precise, slightly cool diction; the wonder is in the facts, not in adjectives. Ethos is the specialist's candid mastery, including candor about what is unknown. Patient build to a quiet culminating claim; the voice resists drawing a moral, letting meaning emerge from accurate description. Semicolons and colons carry the accumulation.
- How it differs: from The Long Think, by withholding all moral judgment and only rendering. A Long Look that lands any argued claim has drifted into Long Think. The high-burst rhythm (short flat against long list) is its signature. Note: Richard Louv was assigned to The Long Look as his primary home (per the distinctness audit) so no exemplar is shared across the Long Think / Long Look pair.

## Distinctness audit: flagged pairs and how they resolved

An adversarial review checked nine register pairs. Six are cleanly distinct on stance, rhythm, and punctuation at once. Two were flagged as real but underpoliced and were hardened with binary gates. Record below.

- The Reckoning vs The Tribute (highest near-merge risk): partially verified. They share nearly identical craft machinery (periodic build to a button, antithesis as engine, anaphoric accumulation) and are separated mainly by vector and closing move, not by measurable syntax. Resolution: keep both, but hard-gate the separation. The Reckoning MUST close on an imperative or charge and MUST carry a nameable culprit plus a datable trigger; "no nameable opposing party" is a hard disqualifier that auto-routes the piece to Tribute or Ledger. The Tribute MUST close on a benediction or placement-in-history, is FORBIDDEN an imperative close, runs lower device density, and bans the fronted-statistic-as-moral-fact move.
- The Long Think vs The Long Look (fuzziest after Reckoning/Tribute): partially verified. Heavy overlap on cumulative-sentence craft, and the two originally shared the Richard Louv exemplar, a concrete smell. Resolution: make "commits to a position or reframes a question" the gate for Long Think versus "withholds all moral judgment and only renders" for Long Look; a Long Look that lands an argued claim has drifted. Reassign Louv exclusively to The Long Look. Make the short-flat-declarative snap a Long Look signature that The Long Think uses sparingly.
- The Long Think vs The Witness Stand: verified and measurable. The Long Think "I" only reasons; the Witness Stand "I" is a memoirist whose lived scene is the evidence. Hardening: a first-person remembered scene starring the author inside a Long Think is an automatic register violation.
- The Ledger vs The Long Look: verified, the cleanest impersonal pair. Separated by burstiness (even, low-variance survey versus high-burst short-against-long) and by punctuation profile, and by stance (adjudicate toward a verdict versus illuminate without a moral). Used as the template for tightening the weaker pairs.
- The Ledger vs The Long Think: verified. Self absent versus present-as-reasoner, plus even rhythm versus high burstiness, plus claim-first versus withheld-turn architecture. Routing question: if the thesis arrives in sentence one and is then supported, it is Ledger; if the piece reasons to an earned turn mid-stride, it is Long Think.
- The Reckoning vs The Open Letter: verified. The power vector is inverted (petition up versus charge the room) and the addressee count differs (one named party versus an assembly). Hardening: The Open Letter keeps a named second-person addressee throughout; a diffuse "we" makes it a Reckoning.
- The Tribute vs The Witness Stand: verified. Both share narrative-into-claim and a gift-close, but the locus of the "I" inverts. Discriminator: whose life supplies the anecdotes. Self-deprecation in a Tribute is flagged as a leak.
- The Open Letter vs The Tribute: verified. Suasion of a pressured addressee versus consecration with no party pressured. No fix required.
- The Reckoning vs The Long Think: verified. Irony is permitted in The Long Think and banned in The Reckoning; audience size (peer versus assembly) and a datable trigger versus a perennial theme separate them.
