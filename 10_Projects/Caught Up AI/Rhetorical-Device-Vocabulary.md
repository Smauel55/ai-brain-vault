# Rhetorical Device Vocabulary (controlled list)

The fixed set of rhetorical devices the product is allowed to name and highlight in a
lesson opener. Set by Samuel on 2026-06-05 after early drafts invented loose labels
("the level close", "comparison that sets scale") and mislabeled at least one device
(calling mid-clause parallelism "anaphora").

## The rule

- Label and highlight devices using ONLY the terms below. No invented or paraphrased
  device names.
- Tag a device only where it is genuinely present. Do not force a label onto a phrase to
  fill a quota. Fewer correct labels beat more loose ones.
- Be precise about look-alikes. The recurring trap: parallelism vs anaphora. Anaphora
  repeats a word or phrase at the START of successive clauses; if the repeated element
  sits mid-clause, it is parallelism, not anaphora. Same care for metaphor vs simile,
  antithesis vs juxtaposition, asyndeton vs polysyndeton.
- This list is encoded in the PDF generator at
  10_Projects/Caught Up AI/Sample-Briefs/render_briefs.py. Keep the two in sync.

## The list

- Ethos: establishing the speaker's credibility or character to earn trust.
- Pathos: appeal to the audience's emotions.
- Logos: appeal through logic, evidence, and reasoning.
- Diction: deliberate word choice and its connotations.
- Syntax: sentence structure used for effect (length, order, fragmentation).
- Tone: the writer's attitude toward subject or audience.
- Parallelism: repeated grammatical structure across phrases or clauses.
- Anaphora: repeating the same word or phrase at the start of successive clauses.
- Juxtaposition: placing two ideas or images side by side to sharpen contrast.
- Antithesis: contrasting ideas set in balanced, parallel form.
- Rhetorical question: a question posed for effect, not an answer.
- Metaphor: implied comparison treating one thing as another.
- Simile: explicit comparison using "like" or "as."
- Imagery: sensory language that builds a concrete picture.
- Allusion: reference to a known text, person, or event to borrow meaning.
- Hyperbole: deliberate exaggeration for emphasis or irony.
- Irony: a gap between what is said and what is meant, or expected and actual.
- Repetition: recurring words or sounds for emphasis or rhythm.
- Concession and refutation: granting the opposing view, then countering it.
- Anecdote: a short personal story used as evidence or to humanize an argument.
- Epistrophe: repeating a word or phrase at the end of successive clauses.
- Asyndeton: omitting conjunctions to speed rhythm and pile up items.
- Polysyndeton: deliberately overusing conjunctions to slow pace and weight each item.
- Chiasmus: inverting the structure of two parallel phrases.
- Understatement / litotes: deliberately downplaying for effect, often ironic.
- Analogy: extended comparison mapping a familiar relationship onto an unfamiliar one.
- Personification: assigning human traits to abstractions or objects.
- Cumulative and periodic sentences: front-loading vs withholding the main clause for effect.
- Anecdotal vs statistical evidence: the deliberate choice of which evidence type to reach for.
- Shift / volta: a deliberate turn in tone, argument, or focus.
