# Caught Up AI - Rotating Structural Templates (D2)

The library of structural architectures a daily piece can be built on, plus the rotation scheme that keeps successive pieces from sharing a shape. This replaces the single open/build/close spine in [[Writing-Manual]] section 3. That spine was correct but it was one skeleton; running it every day is itself an AI tell (cross-piece sameness). Here you pick a different skeleton each day.

How this fits the other deliverables:
- [[Style-Palette]] is the voice layer: which register a piece speaks in.
- [[Register-Specs]] is the per-register detail: diction, sentence habits, ethos posture.
- This file is the bones layer: which architecture carries the argument from first line to last.
- [[Cross-Piece-Sameness-Rubric]] is the audit: it checks that a batch actually varied across register, structure, opening, and rhythm. This file is what you follow so it passes.

A piece has three independent choices: a register (voice), a template (bones), and an open/close pairing (entry and exit). They are not locked together. Most templates suit several registers. The rotation scheme at the bottom rotates all of them at once.

## How to read a template

Each template below gives you five things:
- Name: what to call it.
- Suits: which registers from [[Style-Palette]] sit naturally on these bones.
- Open-move options: how to enter. Pick one.
- Engine: the structural device that drives the middle. This is the one load-bearing choice. Run exactly one engine per piece, the whole way through. Do not stack two.
- Close-move options: how to land. Pick one.

The Writing-Manual rule still holds: one structural engine per piece, varied sentence length and shape, final sentence short, zero blocklist items, no em-dashes, no emojis.

## The template library

### Template 1: Concession-then-Rebuttal

- Suits: The Long Think, The Ledger, The Open Letter (the concede-then-bind variant), The Witness Stand.
- Open-move options:
  - Voice the opposing or conventional view first, generously, in its strongest form, so the concession reads as real and not as a strawman setup.
  - State the thesis flatly as a settled-sounding truism, then immediately signal you are about to complicate it.
- Engine: Grant the other side genuine ground for the first third to half of the piece, then pivot on a single hinge (a "Yet", an "Actually, as a matter of fact", a withheld distinction or metaphor). The turn rests on reframing what was conceded, not on dumping new evidence. Everything after the hinge re-reads the conceded material in the new light.
- Close-move options:
  - Close inward on the completed turn of thought, the reframed premise now feeling inevitable.
  - Button with a compressed image or distinction that crystallizes the turn, then stop. No summary, no exhortation.
- Drift cue: you have drifted if the concession is one limp sentence ("Sure, some people disagree") rather than a real, fairly stated case. A reader should briefly wonder which side you are on. If they never wonder, the concession was fake and the template collapsed into straight assertion.

### Template 2: Antithesis-as-Engine

- Suits: The Reckoning, The Tribute, The Long Look, The Long Think.
- Open-move options:
  - Open directly on the controlling opposition in short declaratives (the few versus the many, certainty versus uncertainty, what could have been done versus what was done).
  - Name one side of the opposition flatly as fact, holding the other side back for one beat.
- Engine: One opposition runs the entire piece. Every paragraph advances the same two-sided tension. Common forms: small against large, private profit against public harm, the one against the others, the acted-upon that also acts. The reader should be able to name the two poles after one read.
- Close-move options:
  - Resolve the antithesis quietly, letting the favored side win by weight rather than by a raised voice (The Long Look, The Long Think).
  - Land the verdict the opposition has been building toward as the audience's own obligation (The Reckoning, The Tribute).
- Drift cue: you have drifted if a second, unrelated opposition sneaks in midway, or if the two poles stop being genuinely opposed and become merely two topics. One axis, held to the end.

### Template 3: Accumulation under a Repeated Frame

- Suits: every register. This is the most portable architecture, which is exactly why you must not over-use it (see rotation cap below).
- Open-move options:
  - Name the controlling subject or frame in one flat sentence, then begin the run.
  - Front the governing fact or number that the accumulation will substantiate.
- Engine: A run of parallel members under one repeated frame ("It would increase... It would increase... It would add..."; "that we have long laboured... that we have long been looked upon..."; "it is to...; it is to...; it is to..."). The case is made by cumulative weight, not by a single dramatic stroke. Members are usually separated by semicolons or kept as short parallel sentences. The frame can be anaphora (same opening words) or a steadier structural parallel.
- Close-move options:
  - Land the button after the run: a short hard sentence that names what all that weight means.
  - Close on the longest, most freighted member, so the final gesture is the accumulation itself rather than a summary (The Long Look).
- Drift cue: you have drifted if the repetition is decorative rather than cumulative, meaning you could delete any one member and lose nothing. Each member must add a distinct instance. Also: a tricolon on autopilot (always exactly three) is a tell. Use two, or four, or five.

### Template 4: Escalating Exemplification

- Suits: The Long Think, The Tribute, The Reckoning, The Witness Stand.
- Open-move options:
  - Open on a single small concrete instance, fully grounded, before any generalization.
  - Name the abstraction you intend to fill, then immediately give the first, smallest example.
- Engine: A sequence of examples that grow in scale or stakes (one person, then a city, then a country; one quiet refusal, then a movement). The escalation is the structure: each example is bigger or more consequential than the last, and the generalization arrives only after the examples have been seen. Distinct from Accumulation in that the members are not parallel-equal; they climb.
- Close-move options:
  - Land a quiet figurative clinch once the ladder tops out (The Long Think's cookie-cutter image).
  - Set the topmost example into permanence or legacy (The Tribute), or into a charge to the audience (The Reckoning).
- Drift cue: you have drifted if the examples are the same size, in which case you are running Accumulation, not Escalation. The reader should feel the ground widen with each one.

### Template 5: Narrative-into-Claim

- Suits: The Tribute, The Witness Stand, The Long Look (the cooler description-into-meaning variant).
- Open-move options:
  - Open mid-scene, present and concrete: a moment, a place, a named hour, a body doing one thing.
  - Name the occasion and immediately claim it as personal or particular before widening out.
- Engine: A small concrete anecdote or scene runs first and is allowed to breathe, then is opened out into its significance ("That moment tells us something about how change happens"). The scene does the persuading; the claim names what the scene already proved. The cool variant (The Long Look) lets meaning emerge from accurate description and resists drawing the moral too hard.
- Close-move options:
  - Collapse the anecdote into one short portable sentence the reader can carry.
  - Place the scene into a longer history or transcendent frame (The Tribute's legacy lift), or hand its meaning to the reader as inheritance.
- Drift cue: you have drifted if the claim arrives before the scene has earned it, or if the scene is generic (could be anyone, anywhere). Pathos lives in specific nouns. A named place and a named hour beat any adjective.

### Template 6: Problem-Reframe (Redefinition-as-Engine)

- Suits: The Long Think, The Witness Stand, The Long Look.
- Open-move options:
  - Name the word or problem everyone thinks they understand (freedom, bravery, hunger, home, the situation as commonly framed).
  - State the common framing as if you accept it, plainly, so the re-specification later feels like a discovery.
- Engine: Take a term or a problem the reader thinks is settled and quietly re-specify what it means, so the argument is won at the level of definition rather than evidence. The whole middle is the re-definition, tested against the common meaning at each step. Reason forward from a premise the reader already shares toward a conclusion they resist.
- Close-move options:
  - Hand the redefined word back to the reader transformed, as a gift or a wish (The Witness Stand's "I wish you Hunger").
  - Land the earned conclusion as a quiet restatement of the reframed premise, the inversion now feeling inevitable (The Long Think).
- Drift cue: you have drifted if you argued the reader into the new meaning by force of evidence rather than by showing the word already contained it. The reader should feel they were shown something true, not out-debated.

### Template 7: Dialectic (Reason-Forward from Shared Premises)

- Suits: The Long Think, The Ledger.
- Open-move options:
  - Begin where opponent and reader already agree, stated as common ground neither side disputes.
  - Name a shared premise and immediately ground it in a concrete consequence, so the premise is felt before it is argued.
- Engine: Start from agreement, then show that the agreed premise compels a conclusion the reader currently resists. Each step is a small, hard-to-refuse move from the last ("Because freedom of discussion improves our own opinions, the liberties of other men are our own vital necessity"). The persuasion is in the chain, not in any single claim. Distinct from Concession-then-Rebuttal: there is no opposing case to grant, only a shared starting point to follow to its end.
- Close-move options:
  - Land the earned conclusion as a quiet restatement of the shared premise, now seen to demand more than the reader expected.
  - Button with the distinction that the whole chain produced, and let it rest.
- Drift cue: you have drifted if any step requires a premise the reader has not already granted. If you have to argue for a link, the chain broke; find the shared ground first.

### Template 8: Withheld-Turn

- Suits: The Witness Stand, The Long Think, The Open Letter.
- Open-move options:
  - Name what the audience expects you to do or say next ("So I will not give you advice").
  - Set up the conventional move so clearly that the reader leans into it, then refuse it.
- Engine: Name the expected move, decline it, and substitute something more personal or more pointed. The refusal is the structure: the piece runs on the gap between what the form promised and what you deliver instead. Often paired with an anaphoric run that confides ("I wish you ___", "I bravely ___") in place of the expected argument.
- Close-move options:
  - Deliver the substituted thing as a wish or blessing the reader is free to take or leave.
  - Return to the opening expectation, now meaning something different than it did at the start.
- Drift cue: you have drifted if you set up the expectation but then deliver roughly what was expected anyway. The substitution has to genuinely surprise the form it sits in.

### Template 9: Periodic-Build-to-a-Button

- Suits: The Reckoning, The Open Letter, The Tribute, The Long Look.
- Open-move options:
  - Begin a long suspended runway of subordinate context, anaphoric clauses loading the situation ("when we are confronted... when we are devoting... when we are asking...").
  - Open on the precipitating event in a long periodic sentence whose main verb arrives late.
- Engine: One or more long periodic sentences hold the main clause (the point, the offense, the verdict) to the very end, loading context first so the landing carries maximum weight. The held main clause is then immediately followed by a short, flat, hard sentence that lands the consequence. The contrast between the long suspended runway and the short button is the whole effect.
- Close-move options:
  - The short hard button after the long sentence ("In the last 24 hours we had their answer."; "I must not be misunderstood.").
  - A short benediction or button in the warm-formal variant (The Tribute), the long cadence resolving into one weighty short line.
- Drift cue: you have drifted if the long sentence is merely long rather than periodic (main clause delayed). If the point arrives early and the rest is trailing detail, you have a loose sentence, not a periodic one, and the button has nothing to land against.

### Template 10: Finding-First / Claim-First Build

- Suits: The Ledger, The Reckoning, The Long Look.
- Open-move options:
  - Name the situation, finding, or verdict flatly and impersonally in sentence one, key actor and often a figure or date already present.
  - Name the breach or the result flatly as fact, with the loaded judgment-noun deferred to the end of the sentence.
- Engine: State the conclusion first, then spend the entire body substantiating it. The structure is inverted from the templates that build to a reveal: there is no withheld turn here, only a flat claim and a steady, ordered case for it (situation, cause, scope, consequence, status). Suits registers whose authority comes from level, on-the-record substantiation rather than from drama.
- Close-move options:
  - State the implication the evidence supports, level and unembellished, in the same even tone as the rest.
  - Leave the matter in its current status, declining to resolve what the record has not resolved. Avoid the button; close on a sentence that could be the topic sentence of one more paragraph (The Ledger).
- Drift cue: you have drifted if you start manufacturing suspense or holding back the finding for effect. This template's honesty is that it tells you the answer up front; if you find yourself teasing, you have switched templates.

### Template 11: Statistic-to-Scene Pivot (sub-architecture)

- Suits: The Reckoning, The Ledger, The Long Look. Often nested inside Antithesis-as-Engine or Accumulation rather than run alone for a full piece.
- Open-move options:
  - Front a governing number as established moral fact, then immediately render it as one human body in a named place at a named hour.
  - Lead with a quantified fact that sets scale, letting the number do the work an adjective would do in a hotter register.
- Engine: Move repeatedly between the aggregate (a statistic, a scale) and the single concrete instance (one person, one scene), then generalize back out. The pivot from number to body to number is the recurring beat. This keeps logos and pathos braided rather than siloed in a data paragraph.
- Close-move options:
  - End on the human scene, the number now felt rather than merely known.
  - End on the restored aggregate, the scale now carrying the weight the single scene gave it.
- Drift cue: you have drifted if the numbers and the scenes live in separate paragraphs that never touch. The point of this template is the pivot; if there is no pivot, you just have facts next to anecdotes.

### Template 12: Satiric Ventriloquism (sub-architecture, rare slot)

- Suits: The Long Think only, and only occasionally. Use sparingly; it is a specialist tool, not a daily one.
- Open-move options:
  - Adopt the target's register and vocabulary straight, with no authorial wink, as if you mean it.
  - Open on the target's own kind of claim, pitched just slightly past where they would pitch it.
- Engine: Imitate the target's voice (jargon, claim-density, self-seriousness) without comment and let it collapse under its own weight. The imitation is the entire critique. No narrator steps in to explain the joke. The reader supplies the judgment.
- Close-move options:
  - End on the single most damning straight-faced detail (a testimonial, an absurd number), trusting the reader to land the verdict.
- Drift cue: you have drifted the moment you wink at the reader or break the deadpan. If the piece signals "this is satire", the device failed. Also: current-events accuracy still binds; do not invent facts to make the satire land.

## The open and close typology (mix-and-match)

Opens and closes are not bolted to one template. Within what a template allows, you can swap entry and exit moves to keep the batch varied. The four broad open types and four broad close types, drawn from the corpus:

Open types:
- Flat-fact / finding-first: name the situation, number, or verdict impersonally in sentence one.
- Concede-first: voice the opposing or conventional view generously before turning.
- In-scene: open mid-moment in a concrete particular (a place, an hour, a body, a kitchen, a courtroom).
- Address / occasion: a vocative or a naming of the present ceremonial or precipitating moment (the speech and letter registers).

Close types:
- Button: a short hard sentence after a long one; the verdict or consequence, compressed.
- Inward turn: the last sentence completes the mind's movement, no call to action.
- Charge / obligation: the verdict handed to the audience as their duty (The Reckoning) or a concrete deadline handed to an addressee (The Open Letter).
- Placement-into-permanence / benediction: the subject set into legacy or a quiet blessing (The Tribute), or left in its current status (The Ledger).

Hard rule carried from [[Writing-Manual]]: the final sentence is short, and the close is never a tidy three-point restatement, never "in conclusion", never a manufactured uplift the piece did not earn.

## The rotation scheme

The goal is that any reader scanning a week of pieces sees four things change day to day: register (voice), template (bones), opening type, and rhythm. A no-code operator can run this by hand from the grid below. No tooling, no formula. Just read across the row for today.

### The rule in one line

Never repeat yesterday's register, never repeat yesterday's template, and never open two days running with the same open type. That is the whole discipline. The grid is just a pre-baked way to guarantee it.

### The 7-day rotation grid

Run this Monday to Sunday, then repeat. If you publish fewer than 7 days a week, keep moving down the grid on each publishing day (do not reset to Monday): skipping rows is fine, repeating a row two pieces in a row is not.

| Day | Register (voice) | Template (bones) | Open type | Rhythm target |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (Mon) | The Reckoning | Periodic-Build-to-a-Button (9) | Address / occasion | Long suspended runway, hard short button |
| 2 (Tue) | The Long Think | Concession-then-Rebuttal (1) | Concede-first | Even, then a single sharp hinge sentence |
| 3 (Wed) | The Ledger | Finding-First / Claim-First (10) | Flat-fact | Level throughout, short flat closer |
| 4 (Thu) | The Witness Stand | Narrative-into-Claim (5) | In-scene | Warm, conversational, one portable short line |
| 5 (Fri) | The Tribute | Antithesis-as-Engine (2) | Address / occasion | Balanced periodic, quiet benediction |
| 6 (Sat) | The Long Look | Accumulation under a Repeated Frame (3) | Flat-fact | Short declarative then long elaboration |
| 7 (Sun) | The Open Letter | Withheld-Turn (8) | Concede-first | Deferential build, deadline or request close |

The grid deliberately never pairs the same register with the same template two weeks running: on the next pass, shift each register one template to the right in the library (Reckoning takes template 10, then 2, and so on), or simply re-roll using the selection logic below. The point is variety over a month, not a fixed liturgy.

### If you publish more than 7 distinct pieces, or want fresh pairings

Selection logic you can run by hand for any single piece:
1. Look at the last 3 pieces you published. Note their registers, templates, and open types.
2. Pick a register from [[Style-Palette]] that is not in those last 3.
3. Pick a template from the library that suits that register (the Suits line) and is not in those last 3.
4. Pick an open type the template allows that is not the same as yesterday's.
5. Pick a close type the template allows; vary it from yesterday too where you can.
6. Set a rhythm target by combining the register's habits from [[Register-Specs]] with the template's natural cadence.

That is enough to guarantee no two consecutive pieces share a shape, and that no register, template, or open type repeats inside any 4-piece window.

### The usage caps (the guardrails)

- Accumulation (Template 3) is the most seductive default because it suits every register. Cap it at one piece in any 4. If you reach for it a third time in a week, you have drifted toward sameness; pick another.
- No register more than twice in any 7 pieces.
- No template more than twice in any 7 pieces.
- The two rare sub-architectures (Statistic-to-Scene as a full spine, Satiric Ventriloquism) appear at most once a fortnight. They are seasoning.
- Periodic-Build and Withheld-Turn both end on a held or refused beat. Do not run them on consecutive days; the back-to-back "delayed payoff" rhythm is itself a sameness tell.

### The drift check (you have drifted if...)

Run this against the last week before you queue the next piece. You have drifted if any of these is true:
- Two consecutive pieces open the same way (two flat-fact opens in a row, two in-scene opens in a row).
- More than two pieces in the last seven run the same template.
- Three or more pieces in a row close on a button. Vary the close type, not just the words.
- Every piece in the week peaks in the same place (all build to a final-line turn, or all front-load the claim). Mix early-claim and late-turn shapes across the week.
- You cannot name, for each of the last seven pieces, a different load-bearing structural device. If two pieces have the same answer, one of them needs rebuilding.

Hand this same week to [[Cross-Piece-Sameness-Rubric]] for the formal audit. This drift check is the fast pre-screen; that rubric is the full pass.
