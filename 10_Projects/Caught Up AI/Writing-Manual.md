# Caught Up AI - Source-Article Writing Manual

The spec for writing (or editing AI-drafted) the daily nonfiction article. Reverse-engineered from the released AP English Language exam corpus (2005-2024, College Board free-response passages, verbatim where available). Goal: produce a 350-450 word argument that sits in the same register an AP Lang teacher already marks up on the exam, takes a clear position, and is rich enough to annotate for rhetorical devices, while reading as human-written, not AI-generated.

This is the apparatus. It is the IP. Treat it as the editorial standard every piece must pass.

## 1. What the exam corpus actually is

The rhetorical-analysis question (Q2) gives students a self-contained nonfiction passage with a clear speaker, audience, occasion, and purpose, and asks them to analyze how the writer achieves that purpose. Verified examples from released exams, the study set to internalize:

| Year | Author | Form | Signature move |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2006 | William Hazlitt, "On the Want of Money" | familiar essay | relentless cumulative syntax, anaphora ("it is to...") |
| 2007 | Scott Russell Sanders, "Staying Put" | essay | concession then rebuttal; one withheld metaphor at the turn |
| 2008 | John M. Barry, "The Great Influenza" | narrative nonfiction | antithesis (certainty vs uncertainty), short declaratives |
| 2010 | Benjamin Banneker, letter to Jefferson | letter | quotes the Declaration back at its author; appeal to conscience |
| 2011 | Florence Kelley, child-labor speech | speech | "Tonight while we sleep" present-tense pivot; concrete child imagery |
| 2012 | John F. Kennedy, steel-price statement | speech | ordinary citizens vs "a tiny handful of steel executives"; statistics |
| 2013 | Richard Louv, "Last Child in the Woods" | essay | irony, anecdote, nostalgic tonal shift at the close |
| 2014 | Abigail Adams, letter to her son | letter | maternal ethos; aphorism ("Great necessities call out great virtues") |
| 2015 | Cesar Chavez, nonviolence article | op-ed | antithesis as engine; aphoristic button |
| 2016 | Margaret Thatcher, Reagan eulogy | eulogy | "Others... He..." anaphora; balanced periodic syntax |
| 2017 | Clare Boothe Luce, press-club speech | speech | disarms a hostile audience by conceding first |
| 2018 | Madeleine Albright, commencement | speech | refrain ("have courage still"); exemplification escalating in scale |
| 2019 | Gandhi, letter to Lord Irwin | letter | measured concessive tone; paradox; ethos of humility |
| 2021 | Obama, Rosa Parks statue dedication | speech | narrative, anecdote, antithesis |
| 2024 | Reshma Saujani, "American Like Me" | memoir | perfection vs bravery antithesis; self-deprecation |

Two corrections worth carrying: the Chavez nonviolence passage is the 2015 Q2 (not a 2014 argument prompt), and these famous mentor essays (Orwell, Didion, Staples, Carson, MLK "Letter from Birmingham Jail") are teaching staples, not exam passages. The exam deliberately avoids the most-anthologized pieces.

The throughline: these passages were chosen because their architecture is legible. The rhetorical choices are visible at sentence level, repeated enough to notice, and tied to an evident purpose. That legibility is what we are manufacturing.

## 2. The target spec

- Length: 350-450 words. A complete rhetorical arc, not a fragment. The exam runs 450-900; we compress the same arc into less room, so density matters more, not less.
- Form: a current op-ed / argument essay by a credible authorial voice. There is no famous speaker, so the "occasion" is a current news hook and the "ethos" is a specific, credible vantage point.
- Position: one clear, arguable thesis the writer obviously believes. No both-sides hedging. The exam needs a legible purpose; so do we.
- Topic: classroom-safe, non-partisan, strong. Avoid electoral and culture-war flashpoints. A pointed argument about attention, work, money, language, ambition, or media is the sweet spot.

## 3. The architecture (open, build, close)

Every piece runs this three-part spine. It is the skeleton a teacher charts.

OPEN (first 1-2 sentences). Set stance or situation fast. No throat-clearing. Pick one:
- A flat fact or understatement you will detonate later (Kelley opens on a statistic).
- A direct naming of the situation or the reader's assumption (Sanders names the "national mythology" he is about to dismantle).
- A bold aphoristic claim (Barry: "Certainty creates strength.").
The first sentence should preview the tone.

BUILD (middle, the bulk). Run exactly one legible structural device as the engine. Do not stack four. Pick one:
- Accumulation under a repeated frame (Kelley's state-by-state tour; Hazlitt's "it is to...").
- Antithesis as engine (Kennedy: citizens vs executives; held across the whole piece).
- Concession then rebuttal (Sanders, Luce: grant the other side real ground, then turn).
- Exemplification escalating in scale (Albright: one city, then a country, then a continent).
The middle is where you put the device a student can underline.

CLOSE (last 1-2 sentences). Engineer the landing. Pick one:
- A call to action (Kelley: "freeing the children from toil").
- A tonal shift or turn inward (Louv ends quiet and nostalgic).
- An aphoristic button (Chavez: "the rich may have money, but the poor have time").
The last sentence is almost always shorter and punchier than what precedes it. End on a short one.

## 4. Sentence rhythm (the single most teachable feature)

The signature of the whole corpus: long periodic or cumulative sentences set against short declaratives. Pile clause on clause, then snap the reader to attention with a four-word sentence. This contrast controls pacing and emphasis, and it is the first thing a teacher marks.

- Vary sentence length deliberately. If three sentences in a row are the same length, break one.
- Earn your short sentences. A short sentence must land a concrete turn, not state an abstract platitude. "But no matter." works because it performs cultural amnesia. "Change is hard." does not, it is filler.
- Use one genuinely long sentence per piece that accumulates, so the short ones have something to cut against.

## 5. The device palette (earned, never decorative)

Use these. They are the recurring devices across the corpus and the ones a teacher version will mark. The rule: every device must do work tied to the purpose. If you can delete it and lose nothing, it was decorative.

- Antithesis / juxtaposition. The most common engine. Set two things against each other and hold the tension.
- Anaphora / repetition. Powerful but easy to overdo. One repeated frame per piece, maximum, and only if it builds force.
- Concrete imagery. Pathos lives in specific nouns, not adjectives. "A pail of midnight luncheon" beats "the hardship workers face."
- Concession. Grant the other side something real before you turn. It builds ethos and makes the rebuttal land.
- Allusion. One well-placed reference (historical, literary, cultural) that the reader does not need outside knowledge to follow.
- Irony / understatement. A deadpan line carrying critique underneath.
- Rhetorical question. Sparingly, and pointed. One per piece at most. AI overuses these as throat-clearing; the corpus uses them to pivot.

Appeals, how the corpus braids them:
- Ethos: established early, by how the writer presents their vantage. For an op-ed, that is a specific, credible angle, not a resume.
- Pathos: rides on one concrete, particular detail, not on abstraction or emotional adjectives.
- Logos: interleaved with the other two, not siloed in a "data paragraph." The best sentences do two appeals at once.

## 6. The markability test

Before a piece passes, confirm a teacher could annotate it. Ask:
- Is there a clear speaker stance, an implied audience, and an evident purpose?
- Is the structural device repeated enough to be noticeable, and underline-able?
- Can a student point to a specific sentence and name what it does and why?
- Does it reward close reading without requiring outside knowledge?
If a piece is well-written but has no legible architecture, it fails. It must be both good prose and good teaching material.

## 7. The AI-tell blocklist (hard rules)

A piece is rejected if it contains any of these. This is what makes the blind test winnable.

Banned punctuation and constructions:
- Em-dashes. Hard rule, no exceptions (Samuel's voice standard anyway). Use commas, colons, periods, or parentheses.
- The "It's not just X, it's Y" / "isn't merely X, it's Y" reveal cadence. Note: genuine antithesis is encouraged (section 5). The banned thing is this specific AI sing-song template and "not only... but also" filler. Real antithesis sets two ideas against each other to make a point; the tell uses the shape to manufacture false depth.
- "In a world where...", "In today's world", "In an age of..." openers.
- "Here's the thing", "Here's the kicker", "But here's the catch".
- "Let's dive in", "Let's unpack", "Let's explore".
- "At the end of the day", "When all is said and done".
- "more than ever", "now more than ever".
- Sweeping "from X to Y" range gestures ("from the boardroom to the classroom").
- A tidy bow conclusion that restates the three points just made.

Banned diction (AI-favored vocabulary):
- delve, tapestry, testament, underscore, pivotal, crucial, vital, robust, nuanced, multifaceted, holistic, paradigm, myriad, plethora, foster, leverage (as a verb), resonate, navigate (figurative), landscape (figurative), realm, beacon, embark, journey (figurative), seamless, ever-evolving, fast-paced, game-changer.

Banned structure:
- Every sentence the same length (kills the rhythm in section 4).
- Monotone syntax, even when the lengths vary. Length alone is not enough. If every sentence shares the same grammatical shape, the piece still reads as a machine wrote it. The usual tell is starting clause after clause on the subject (noun, verb, object, then repeat), or leaning on one connector to bolt clauses together. Blend the syntax on purpose: mix simple, compound, complex, and compound-complex sentences; open some sentences on a dependent clause, an adverb, or a prepositional phrase rather than on the subject; let one sentence run periodic (main clause held to the end) and the next run loose (main clause first); drop a deliberate fragment once or twice where it earns its place. The page should look uneven. That unevenness is most of what reads as a human, and its absence is most of what reads as a bot.
- Relentless symmetry and over-parallelism. Real voice is a little ragged.
- Stacked participial openers ("Having considered... Recognizing that... Understanding the...").
- The fake-profound one-line paragraph stating an abstraction. (A short sentence is good only if it carries concrete weight, see section 4.)
- Vague authority: "studies show", "experts agree", "research suggests", with nothing specific attached.
- Tricolon on autopilot. Not every list is three items. Use two, or four, sometimes.

## 8. Pre-flight checklist (every piece)

1. 350-450 words.
2. One clear, arguable thesis the writer believes.
3. Open sets stance fast, no throat-clearing.
4. Exactly one structural engine in the middle.
5. Close lands; final sentence is short.
6. Sentence variety present in two dimensions: length (long against short) and structure (mixed sentence types and varied openings, not every sentence on the subject).
7. At least three markable devices, each doing real work.
8. Pathos carried by one concrete particular, not abstraction.
9. Zero items from the section 7 blocklist.
10. No em-dashes, no emojis.
11. Reads like a person with a point of view wrote it in one sitting.

## 9. Generation brief (paste-ready, if AI drafts the first pass)

Write a 350-450 word op-ed arguing [THESIS]. One clear position you obviously believe; no both-sides hedging. Open by naming the assumption you are about to overturn or stating a flat fact you will detonate, no throat-clearing. In the middle, run a single structural engine the whole way: [antithesis / accumulation under a repeated frame / concession-then-rebuttal / escalating examples]. Carry the emotional weight on one concrete, specific detail, a real scene or number, not on abstraction or feeling-words. Vary sentence length hard: at least one long accumulating sentence, several short punchy ones, and end on a short line that lands. Vary sentence structure as well as length: mix simple, compound, and complex sentences, and do not start every sentence on the subject; open some on a dependent clause or a prepositional phrase, and use a deliberate fragment once if it earns its place. Use real rhetorical devices (antithesis, one earned repetition, concrete imagery, a concession, maybe one pointed rhetorical question) but only where they do work.

Hard bans: no em-dashes. No "it's not just X, it's Y". No "in a world where", "here's the thing", "at the end of the day", "more than ever", "from X to Y". None of these words: delve, tapestry, testament, underscore, pivotal, crucial, vital, robust, nuanced, navigate, landscape, realm, foster, leverage, resonate, seamless, myriad. No tidy three-point summary conclusion. No relentless symmetry. Sound like one opinionated person wrote it in one sitting, not like a model balancing every clause.
