# Production Generation Briefs (D7)

The paste-ready drafting layer. Everything upstream (the palette, the bands, the templates, the tell list, the accuracy protocol) exists so that this file can be used without re-reading any of it on a given day. Each register below is a self-contained prompt you paste to write that day's piece in that voice. Below the briefs sits the master orchestration note (how to choose register and template so a week never reads like one writer) and the per-piece pre-flight checklist (the four gates a piece clears before it ships).

How this fits the other deliverables:
- [[Style-Palette]] decides which register fits the news. Read it when routing a story.
- [[Register-Specs]] holds the full qualitative craft and the measurable bands for each voice. The briefs here compress those into a paste-ready prompt; when a band cue is ambiguous, Register-Specs is the authority.
- [[Structural-Templates]] holds the twelve skeletons and the rotation grid. The orchestration note here runs that grid.
- [[Anti-Tell-List]] is the per-piece banned-construction gate. The pre-flight here runs its mechanical pass.
- [[Cross-Piece-Sameness-Rubric]] is the batch-level audit. The orchestration note here fills the Variety Matrix that makes the rubric come back clean.
- [[Accuracy-Guardrail]] is the fact-fidelity gate. The pre-flight here runs its per-piece fact-check.

Two rules sit above every brief and every band, and override any corpus evidence or argument that "the source did it":
- NO em-dashes anywhere. Not the long dash, not an en-dash standing in for one. Commas, colons, semicolons, periods, and parentheses carry every suspension and turn.
- NO emojis anywhere.

A reminder carried from [[Accuracy-Guardrail]]: you draft from a numbered fact list, never from an open source. The register controls how the piece sounds; it never decides whether a fact is true. Never bend a fact to fit a register's shape.

## How to use a brief

1. Route the story to a register using [[Style-Palette]], or take today's register from the rotation grid in the orchestration note below.
2. Build the numbered pre-draft fact list per [[Accuracy-Guardrail]]. Close the sources.
3. Paste the matching brief below. Fill the two slots at its head: the chosen template (from the Suits line) and the chosen open and close moves.
4. Draft from the fact list, inside the brief's bands.
5. Run the per-piece pre-flight checklist at the bottom of this file before the piece joins the batch.

Each brief has the same shape: a one-line identity, a slot header to fill, the voice instruction (what the prose must do), the bands (the measurable targets, stated as plain cues), the templates this register sits on, the open and close repertoire, and the hard bans. The bands are ranges, not bullseyes. Land inside the range and you are in register.

## Brief 1: The Ledger

Paste-ready prompt. Identity: an impersonal, evidence-forward voice where authority belongs to the documented record, not to any speaking self. See [[The Ledger]] in [[Style-Palette]] and the full bands in [[Register-Specs]].

Fill before drafting:
- Template (from the Suits line): __________
- Open move: __________   Close move: __________
- Today's fact list is built and the sources are closed: yes / no

Voice instruction:
- Write as a clearinghouse for the documented record. State what is the case, name who established it, quantify where you can, weigh the competing claims, and let the evidence compel the conclusion. You do take a position, but it arrives as the residue the facts leave behind, never as your conviction.
- Keep the first person near-absent. Institutional "we" at most, never a confessing "I."
- Attribute every load-bearing claim to a named body, figure, or date so the reader could in principle check it. The citation is the credibility.
- Handle the other side by acknowledgment then qualification ("yes, but the data show"). Grant its strongest point before you narrow it.
- Order by logic, not drama: situation, cause, scope, consequence, status.
- Keep device density low. Plainness is the ethos. Pathos lives in a concrete number or a specific human-scale detail that you state and decline to comment on. If you reach for an emotive modifier, you have left the register.
- Repeat the precise noun (the agency, the figure, the program) rather than cycling synonyms.

Bands (plain cues):
- Sentences average 19 to 21 words. Drifted if the prose reads as dense as a government report (routinely past 25) or as thin as a news brief (routinely under 14).
- At least one short declarative reset under 10 words. No sentence past about 44.
- Medium burstiness, calmer than the hotter registers. Most sentences cluster 16 to 28 words with occasional dips to a short verdict line. Drifted if the rhythm swings long-then-two-words for drama.
- Lean loose, not periodic: the workhorse sentence states its point early and trails qualifiers and attributions after it.
- Subject-first about half the time; the rest open on a preposition ("In 2008", "According to officials") or a light adverbial ("Similarly", "Although", "Because"). Drifted if nearly every sentence opens on its grammatical subject.
- Parallelism is structural and occasional (a catalogue of attributed facts), not an anaphoric refrain.
- Latinate-leaning and mid-formal, but the formality is in the precision, never in ornament. Prefer the plain Anglo-Saxon verb where one exists.
- Readable in one pass: roughly college level, not government-report dense.

Templates this register sits on: Finding-First / Claim-First Build (10, the home template), Concession-then-Rebuttal (1), Dialectic (7), Accumulation under a Repeated Frame (3), Statistic-to-Scene Pivot (11). See [[Structural-Templates]].

Open repertoire: name the situation or finding flatly with a key actor and often a figure or date already in sentence one; or open with the contested question stated neutrally; or lead with a quantified fact that sets scale; or establish the stakes institutionally.

Close repertoire: state the implication the evidence supports, level and unembellished; or leave the matter in its current status; or land a quiet qualified verdict phrased as conclusion, not exhortation. No call to action, no uplift, no image. If the last line feels like a mic-drop, rewrite it as a status report.

Hard bans for this register: no em-dashes, no emojis, no rhetorical questions, no flourish close, no emotive modifiers standing in for a fact, no significance-inflation ("underscores the importance of", "marks a turning point"), no copula-avoidance ("serves as" where "is" is honest). The full per-piece list is [[Anti-Tell-List]].

## Brief 2: The Open Letter

Paste-ready prompt. Identity: one voice addressing one more-powerful named party, deference on the surface and moral pressure underneath, with the public invited to overhear. See [[The Open Letter]] in [[Style-Palette]] and the full bands in [[Register-Specs]].

Fill before drafting:
- Template (from the Suits line): __________
- Open move: __________   Close move: __________
- Named addressee (a real, specific power held throughout): __________
- Today's fact list is built and the sources are closed: yes / no

Voice instruction:
- Address one named, more-powerful party directly and in the second person throughout. The public is overhearing, not being spoken to.
- Lower your own standing explicitly, then make the addressee's own stated principles, not your person, carry the weight. The signature move is to quote their words or professed principles back at them so the pressure looks like their idea.
- Build long periodic sentences whose stacked subordination performs the deference and enacts the care of the thinking. Open on a concessive or subordinate clause and suspend the main verb at least once in the piece. A colon, not a dash, marks the turn into the main claim.
- Use a concession-then-bind move: grant the addressee's good faith, then make refusal a betrayal of that good faith.
- Argue in the abstract, then let one or two concrete images carry the feeling. Pathos is rationed into a few charged nouns and a scene, not spread through the piece.
- Earnest throughout. Irony and sarcasm read as insolence here and forfeit the deference. A rhetorical question is allowed only where indictment is licensed, and at most once.

Bands (plain cues):
- Blend the poles: aim for an average around 24 to 34 words a sentence. Do not write the 80-plus-word museum sentences of the oldest exemplar; that reads as pastiche.
- At least one genuinely long periodic sentence (the suspended showpiece) and at least one short flat landing sentence under 12 words. Drifted if three sentences in a row take the same breath.
- High burstiness: the long suspended sentence answered by a short flat one is the fingerprint.
- Lean periodic relative to ordinary prose: at least one fully periodic sentence, main clause withheld to the end.
- At least one sustained parallel or anaphoric series per piece, tricolon the preferred shape ("that we have long... that we have long... that we have long").
- Semicolons do heavy structural work, separating the coordinate members of the long suspensions. Commas carry the appositives. Use a colon for the turn into the main claim, never a dash.
- Latinate and formal in the reasoning clauses, plain Anglo-Saxon in the emotional beats and the short landings.
- Abstract-leaning overall (rights, duty, justice, conviction) with one to three concrete image-anchors.

Templates this register sits on: Withheld-Turn (8, the home template), Periodic-Build-to-a-Button (9), Concession-then-Rebuttal (1, the concede-then-bind variant), Accumulation under a Repeated Frame (3). See [[Structural-Templates]].

Open repertoire: a direct vocative that flatters station and stakes a claim at once; or an apology for the liberty of writing that doubles as a statement of your lower standing and higher purpose; or a warm salutation where kinship exists; or anchor to the precipitating event in the first sentence or two.

Close repertoire: a renewed deference that frames your request as thrown on the addressee's better nature; or a concrete deadline or condition that hands the next move to them, making the consequence their responsibility, not your threat; or a self-lowering subscription in the formal variant.

Hard bans for this register: no em-dashes, no emojis, no sarcasm or irony, no diffuse "we" or "you all" addressee (that is a Reckoning, not an Open Letter), no more than one rhetorical question, no significance-inflation, no copula-avoidance. Full list in [[Anti-Tell-List]].

## Brief 3: The Reckoning

Paste-ready prompt. Identity: a speaker with moral or institutional standing pressing a gathered audience toward feeling and action, turning a shared principle into an accusation the reader cannot dodge. See [[The Reckoning]] in [[Style-Palette]] and the full bands in [[Register-Specs]].

Fill before drafting:
- Template (from the Suits line): __________
- Open move: __________   Close move: __________
- The nameable opposing handful, and the live datable trigger that licenses the charge: __________
- Today's fact list is built and the sources are closed: yes / no

Voice instruction:
- Stand on a moral floor the reader already shares and refuse to let the reader stand apart from the wrong. Be prosecutorial but inclusive: a "we" is implicated even as a nameable "few" is charged. Put yourself inside the verdict ("the American people will find it hard, as I do, to accept") so the accusation reads as conscience, not attack.
- Reserve this register for stories where the facts genuinely support attributing responsibility to a nameable handful, and where a live, datable trigger licenses the urgency. Without that trigger the register curdles into a sermon.
- Fuse fact and feeling inside single sentences. The statistic is the emotional payload: render a number as one human body in a real place at a real hour, then generalize back out.
- Build a long suspended runway, usually anaphoric, that loads public context ("when we are confronted... when we are asking..."), then drop the main clause that names the offense and its scale. Follow the enormous sentence with a short, hard consequence sentence.
- Drive the piece on one antithesis: the many against the few, private profit against public harm.
- Close on a charge to act handed to the audience as their duty, never on a meditation or a both-sides softening.

Bands (plain cues):
- Sentences average 22 to 28 words. Long enough to sustain a suspended runway, short enough to land buttons.
- Exactly one or two deliberate runways of 45 to 70 words, against buttons of 5 to 9 words. Drifted if every sentence is roughly the same length; the long-against-short contrast is the heartbeat.
- High burstiness, concentrated: most sentences sit in a medium band, then one balloons into the runway and one collapses to a button right after.
- Compound-complex dominant; the simple sentences are the buttons and the flat accusations.
- At least one sustained anaphoric or parallel series of three or more members ("when we... when we... when we"; "It would increase... It would increase... It would add"). Under-using parallelism is the most common way to fall out of this register.
- Comma-dense from the long parallel series. A rhetorical question only as indictment, never idle musing, and at most one. Carry the suspension on "when... when..." clauses, semicolons, and commas, never a dash.
- Judgment vocabulary leans Latinate ("unjustifiable", "irresponsible defiance"); the human scenes and buttons lean short and Anglo-Saxon. The contrast is intentional.
- Cluster devices in the runway and the accumulation, then go bare in the buttons. Even device distribution is itself a machine tell.

Templates this register sits on: Periodic-Build-to-a-Button (9, the home template), Antithesis-as-Engine (2), Escalating Exemplification (4), Statistic-to-Scene Pivot (11), Accumulation under a Repeated Frame (3). See [[Structural-Templates]].

Open repertoire: name the breach flatly in sentence one with the loaded judgment-noun deferred to the end; or front the governing statistic as established moral fact; or set the live occasion and timestamp so urgency is established before any argument.

Close repertoire: a short, hard button after a long sentence; or an explicit charge to the audience framed as collective duty; or a restatement of the principle as the audience's own obligation. Never a meditation, never a qualification, never a both-sides softening.

Hard bans for this register: no em-dashes, no emojis, no sarcasm, no rhetorical question except the single indicting one, no false balance, no significance-inflation. The charge must rest on facts that support attributing responsibility; do not manufacture a villain the record does not name. Full list in [[Anti-Tell-List]].

## Brief 4: The Tribute

Paste-ready prompt. Identity: a dignified voice consecrating a person or moment, binding a single life or gesture to shared values, the speaker receding behind the honored subject. See [[The Tribute]] in [[Style-Palette]] and the full bands in [[Register-Specs]].

Fill before drafting:
- Template (from the Suits line): __________
- Open move: __________   Close move: __________
- The occasion (death, anniversary, milestone, award, farewell) and the subject honored: __________
- Today's fact list is built and the sources are closed: yes / no

Voice instruction:
- Take a settled, reverent posture toward a subject whose worth you treat as already established. The job is to articulate and elevate it, never to prove it against an opponent. The vector is upward and inward, the opposite of The Reckoning's outward thrust.
- Recede behind the subject. First person appears mainly to register debt or affection.
- Produce grandeur by restraint, not by piling on superlatives. Understatement is the governing device: the smallness of the means magnifies the largeness of the result.
- Run a small concrete anecdote first, then open it out into significance (the bus seat becomes "how change happens"). Let the scene do the honoring; the claim names what the scene already proved.
- Use balanced, cadenced syntax meant to be spoken slowly, with antithesis of the small and the large ("slight in stature but mighty in courage") and an anaphoric refrain that sets the figure apart.
- Close on quiet elevation, the figure placed in a longer history or a short benediction. Never a call to action.

Bands (plain cues):
- Sentences average 16 to 20 words.
- At least one sentence under 6 words (the button) and at least one over 35 (the cadenced accumulation).
- High burstiness: pair very long cadenced sentences against very short declarative buttons and let the contrast do the emotional work. Drifted if every sentence is 15 to 22 words.
- A parallel or anaphoric construction roughly every two to four sentences. This is the single most distinctive feature; under-using it is the fastest way to drift out of register.
- Open about one sentence in five with a coordinating conjunction ("And", "Yet", "But", "So") for cadence. This is a positive marker here, not an error.
- This register's appositive-accumulation habit naturally reaches for the dash. It is BANNED. Replace every dash function with a comma, a colon, a semicolon, or a sentence break. This is the single most important mechanical adaptation for this voice.
- Plain Anglo-Saxon for the human and concrete beats, Latinate elevation for the consecratory and abstract. The movement from plain to elevated within a paragraph is itself a device.
- Anchor every abstract claim about character to one concrete image (a bus seat, blistered feet, a clutched purse). When the prose floats in pure abstraction (courage, dignity, legacy) with no scene, it has gone sentimental and left the register.

Templates this register sits on: Antithesis-as-Engine (2, the home template), Narrative-into-Claim (5), Escalating Exemplification (4), Accumulation under a Repeated Frame (3), Periodic-Build-to-a-Button (9). See [[Structural-Templates]].

Open repertoire: a direct invocation of the present ceremonial moment plus the subject's defining compression; or a plain declaration of loss or worth in a balanced tricolon; or an understated establishment of the subject by negation, withholding the elevation.

Close repertoire: quiet elevation that places the figure in a longer history and resolves on a short benediction or button; or a scriptural or transcendent lift in the final movement; or a reflexive turn that hands the subject's meaning to the audience as inheritance, stopping short of an imperative.

Hard bans for this register: no em-dashes (highest-risk register for them, scan twice), no emojis, no irony or sarcasm, no rhetorical question, no call to action, no hyperbole or piled superlatives, no significance-inflation. Full list in [[Anti-Tell-List]].

## Brief 5: The Long Think

Paste-ready prompt. Identity: a writer thinking aloud to a peer-reader, exploratory and unhurried, granting the other side real ground before turning. No crowd to move, no power to flatter. See [[The Long Think]] in [[Style-Palette]] and the full bands in [[Register-Specs]].

Fill before drafting:
- Template (from the Suits line): __________
- Open move: __________   Close move: __________
- The durable human theme behind the news this piece turns over (attention, money, place, work, language): __________
- Today's fact list is built and the sources are closed: yes / no

Voice instruction:
- Reason in the open to a single thoughtful peer. Grant the other side real intellectual ground, then arrive at a conclusion that feels earned rather than asserted. Authority is a peer thinking harder than you have, not an expert ranking above you.
- Keep your biography off the page. The "I" here is the I of a reasoner, not a memoirist. No "I remember when," no personal scene starring the author. That belongs to The Witness Stand and is the key thing to keep out.
- Use cumulative loose sentences that discover their point mid-stride, often anaphoric ("it is to...; it is to..."). Concede the opposing view fully, then turn on a single hinge word and a withheld distinction or metaphor.
- Braid the feeling into the reasoning. Pathos lives in exact concrete particulars dropped inside analytic sentences and in one late tonal shift, never in a separate "and that matters because" beat.
- Close inward, on the completed turn of thought. No summary, no "in conclusion", no exhortation to act.
- Satiric mode (rare, at most once a fortnight): adopt the target's own register straight-faced and let the excess indict itself. No authorial wink. Current-events accuracy still binds; do not invent facts to make the satire land.

Bands (plain cues):
- Sentences average 22 to 28 words.
- At least one short button or hinge sentence of 3 to 8 words is mandatory; at most one or two cumulative sentences of 45 to 65 words. Drifted if every sentence is within five words of the mean.
- High burstiness: most sentences in the low twenties, then one long cumulative sentence discovering its point across semicolon-separated members, answered by a short sentence that lands the turn.
- Loose and cumulative dominant; periodic sentences are a deliberate minority concentrated at turns and the close.
- At least one extended parallel or anaphoric series per piece; it is the register's primary rhythm.
- Semicolons are the signature mark, separating the major members of the cumulative sentences. A colon marks the pivot. Render every appositive a dash would set off with commas or parentheses instead.
- Balanced diction: plain strong words for the concrete images, Latinate abstractions only for the argumentative joints. Difficulty comes from idea density and sentence length, never from jargon.
- No abstraction goes more than two sentences without a concrete anchor. The pathos lives entirely in the concrete nouns.

Templates this register sits on: Concession-then-Rebuttal (1, the home template), Dialectic (7), Problem-Reframe (6), Escalating Exemplification (4), Withheld-Turn (8), Antithesis-as-Engine (2), Satiric Ventriloquism (12, rare). See [[Structural-Templates]].

Open repertoire: state the thesis flatly as a settled-sounding truism, then spend the piece complicating it; or voice the opposing view first, generously and in its best form; or name the abstraction you intend to re-examine and immediately ground it in a concrete consequence.

Close repertoire: close inward on a turn of thought; or land the earned conclusion as a quiet restatement of the reframed premise; or button with a compressed image or distinction that crystallizes the turn, and let it rest. In the satiric mode, end on the most damning straight-faced detail.

Hard bans for this register: no em-dashes, no emojis, no first-person biography or personal scene, no throat-clearing rhetorical-question opener (a single genuine probing question mid-piece is allowed), no "Here's the thing" pivots, no significance-coda homily. Full list in [[Anti-Tell-List]].

## Brief 6: The Witness Stand

Paste-ready prompt. Identity: a contemporary first-person voice building authority from lived experience offered up, turning a personal story into a public claim with self-aware warmth. See [[The Witness Stand]] in [[Style-Palette]] and the full bands in [[Register-Specs]].

Fill before drafting:
- Template (from the Suits line): __________
- Open move: __________   Close move: __________
- The shared human experience the story runs through, and the moment that licenses the intimacy: __________
- Today's fact list is built and the sources are closed: yes / no

Voice instruction:
- Stand inside the subject as someone who has lived the thing, then turn to face the reader. Advance claims as earned conclusions ("I learned X") rather than assertions ("X is true"). Be confiding, not commanding.
- Build authority by vulnerability and qualification, not by reciting credentials. Name the personal stake out loud. Self-deprecate to disarm. Confess a failure as proof of having tried. The moral authority comes from having been the small one in the story.
- Note for this product: the first-person scene must be credible and true to the piece's framing. Do not fabricate a personal anecdote to dress up a current-events fact. Use this register only when the news genuinely touches an experience the piece can credibly say it has stood inside; otherwise route the story elsewhere per [[Style-Palette]].
- Run the scene first, let it carry the feeling, then resolve it into a portable idea. Pathos lives in concrete personal nouns and scenes, never in abstract emotional vocabulary.
- Take one ordinary word (bravery, hunger, voice, home) and re-define it against its common meaning as the organizing move.
- Vary sentence length freely, from fragments to looping middles, to mimic a live speaking mind.
- Close by handing the lesson to the reader as a gift or a wish, not a directive.

Bands (plain cues):
- Sentences average 17 to 24 words, toward the lower middle so the long sentences land harder against the short.
- At least one sentence under 8 words (the clipped confession or punchline) and at least one over 35 (the one looping comma-chained middle).
- High burstiness: a live speaking mind. Run one long looping sentence, then drop to a 4-to-6-word line right after it. Avoid three medium sentences in a row.
- Strongly loose-leaning. Reserve one periodic build for a section end or the close.
- One anaphoric run per piece (three or four parallel members under a confiding frame like "I wish you ___"). Outside that run, keep parallelism light.
- One to three deliberate fragments per 400 words, for intimacy, never accidental. Conversational conjunction-openers ("So", "And", "But") are positive markers here.
- Comma-forward and conversational. Parentheses are welcome for the confiding aside. Semicolons rare. The source authors lean on dashes for asides; substitute parentheses, commas, or a colon. One exclamation point allowed for a self-deprecating beat.
- Strongly concrete: specific nameable things from a life. Anglo-Saxon-leaning and spoken; a Latinate word is a deliberate lift, not the baseline.

Templates this register sits on: Narrative-into-Claim (5, the home template), Problem-Reframe (6), Withheld-Turn (8), Escalating Exemplification (4), Concession-then-Rebuttal (1), Accumulation under a Repeated Frame (3). See [[Structural-Templates]].

Open repertoire: name the occasion and immediately claim it as personal; or open mid-scene in your own life, present and concrete; or a self-deprecating disarmer that lowers the stakes before raising them; or a flat confession of smallness or outsider status.

Close repertoire: hand the organizing word back to the reader transformed, as a gift; or collapse the long anecdote into one short portable sentence; or a forward-looking blessing or wish rather than a directive; or return to the opening image now meaning something different.

Hard bans for this register: no em-dashes, no emojis, no rhetorical question used as a hammer, no cold aphorism delivered from authority, no sarcasm or contempt (warmth forbids it), no significance-inflation, no fabricated personal anecdote. Full list in [[Anti-Tell-List]].

## Brief 7: The Long Look

Paste-ready prompt. Identity: an absorbed observer rendering a phenomenon, place, or process with precision and awe, so the prose enacts the complexity it admires rather than arguing a position. See [[The Long Look]] in [[Style-Palette]] and the full bands in [[Register-Specs]].

Fill before drafting:
- Template (from the Suits line): __________
- Open move: __________   Close move: __________
- The genuinely awe-worthy system, place, or process (not a conflict or accountability story): __________
- Today's fact list is built and the sources are closed: yes / no

Voice instruction:
- Report a phenomenon from inside its complexity. Do not argue a position. Earn belief by demonstrating accurate, patient attention. Reserve this register for news that is genuinely awe-worthy; it is the wrong voice for conflict, scandal, blame, or breaking-news urgency.
- Build authority by precision: never say you are an expert, let the density and correctness of the detail do it. The strongest ethos signature is candor about what is not known. Mastery that admits its edges reads as more trustworthy than mastery that performs total command.
- Snap short flat declaratives against long accumulating technical sentences. State a flat fact, then open the next sentence out to carry the complexity. The rhythm contrast is the single most imitable move.
- Use accretive lists whose growing clauses mirror the subject's intricacy ("its size, its sediment load, its depth, variations in its bottom..."). Let a controlling antithesis frame the mystery (certainty against uncertainty, the acted-upon that also acts).
- Keep diction precise and slightly cool. The wonder is in the facts, not in adjectives. Do not assert the wonder; produce it through accurate, mounting detail. The biggest tell to avoid is editorializing wonder-words ("astonishing", "breathtaking") instead of letting the facts be astonishing.
- Build patiently to a quiet culminating claim. Resist drawing the moral; let meaning emerge from accurate description.

Bands (plain cues):
- Sentences average 16 to 24 words; the mean alone is misleading here because the variance is the point.
- At least one sentence under 8 words (the flat declarative) and at least one over 35 (the accumulating technical sentence).
- High burstiness: every long list-heavy sentence answered nearby by a short blunt one. Drifted if consecutive sentences are all 20 to 30 words; that is a flat institutional rhythm.
- Loose and cumulative dominant: main clause first, then a cascade of added detail. Periodic build reserved for the piece-level closer.
- One or two strongly parallel accretive series per piece (the "its X, its Y, its Z" pattern), plus parallel short declaratives at openings.
- Semicolons are the workhorse, joining the accumulating clauses and snapping between paired declaratives where another writer would use a dash. A colon introduces an enumeration. Replace every dash with a semicolon, colon, comma pair, or sentence break.
- Mixed and functionally split diction: Anglo-Saxon and blunt in the short declarative spine, Latinate and precise in the technical accumulation. The contrast is part of the texture.
- Strongly concrete: specific physical nouns and measurable particulars. Abstraction is allowed only as a frame and is cashed out in concrete detail immediately after.
- Controlled repetition (the "its... its..." frame, the antithesis keyword) intentionally lowers local diversity. Do not fight it with forced synonyms.

Templates this register sits on: Accumulation under a Repeated Frame (3, the home template), Antithesis-as-Engine (2), Narrative-into-Claim (5, the cool description-into-meaning variant), Problem-Reframe (6), Periodic-Build-to-a-Button (9), Statistic-to-Scene Pivot (11), Finding-First (10). See [[Structural-Templates]].

Open repertoire: state the subject as a flat declarative, often with a comparative claim that sets up the scale to come; or open on the controlling antithesis in clipped sentences; or begin with a precise concrete particular and widen outward.

Close repertoire: land a quiet, slightly cool culminating sentence that resolves the opening antithesis without moralizing; or close on the longest, most technical accumulation so the final gesture is the complexity itself; or end on a calibrating comparison or a candid limit. Avoid the rousing button, the call to action, and the rhetorical-question closer.

Hard bans for this register: no em-dashes, no emojis, no rhetorical question, no second-person exhortation, no asserted wonder-words doing the work the facts should do, no significance-inflation, no moral drawn for the reader. Full list in [[Anti-Tell-List]].

## Master orchestration note

This is how Samuel picks register and template per piece so a week of pieces never reads like one writer on seven days. It runs the rotation scheme in [[Structural-Templates]] and feeds the Variety Matrix in [[Cross-Piece-Sameness-Rubric]]. No tooling, no formula. Read across one row for today.

The discipline in one line: never repeat yesterday's register, never repeat yesterday's template, and never open two days running with the same open type. Engineer the variety in before you write; the [[Cross-Piece-Sameness-Rubric]] is then confirmation, not your only defense.

### The weekly default (the pre-baked rotation)

Run this Monday to Sunday, then re-roll for the next pass. If you publish fewer than seven days a week, keep moving down the grid on each publishing day rather than resetting to Monday. Skipping rows is fine; repeating a row two pieces running is not. This grid is lifted from [[Structural-Templates]] so the two files agree; that file is the authority if they ever diverge.

| Day | Register (brief to paste) | Template (bones) | Open type | Rhythm target |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 (Mon) | The Reckoning | Periodic-Build-to-a-Button (9) | Address / occasion | Long suspended runway, hard short button |
| 2 (Tue) | The Long Think | Concession-then-Rebuttal (1) | Concede-first | Even, then a single sharp hinge sentence |

On the next week's pass, shift each register one template to the right in the library (so The Reckoning takes template 10, then 2, and so on), or re-roll using the selection logic below. The point is variety over a month, not a fixed liturgy.

### Match register to the news first (routing overrides the grid)

The grid is a starting hand, not a cage. Match the register to the story per [[Style-Palette]], and let routing override the grid when the day's news demands it:
- No nameable opposing party, no live trigger: it is not a Reckoning.
- An occasion to honor rather than argue: it is a Tribute.
- A direct address to a single named power: it is an Open Letter.
- No genuine awe, or a conflict or accountability angle: it is not a Long Look.
- No credible lived experience the piece can stand inside: it is not a Witness Stand.
- A thesis that arrives in sentence one and is then supported: Ledger. A piece that reasons to an earned turn mid-stride: Long Think.

When routing forces a swap, just keep the week's spread intact: do not let the swap put two of the same register or template back to back, and re-balance the rest of the week around it.

### Selection logic for any single piece (run by hand)

1. Look at the last three pieces you published. Note their registers, templates, and open types.
2. Pick a register that fits today's news (per [[Style-Palette]]) and is not in those last three.
3. Pick a template from that register's Suits line (above, or in [[Structural-Templates]]) that is not in those last three.
4. Pick an open type the template allows that is not the same as yesterday's.
5. Pick a close type the template allows; vary it from yesterday too where you can.
6. Set a rhythm target by combining the register's bands (above) with the template's natural cadence.

That guarantees no two consecutive pieces share a shape and no register, template, or open type repeats inside any four-piece window.

### The guardrails (caps carried from the templates file)

- Accumulation (template 3) suits every register and is therefore the seductive default. Cap it at one piece in any four.
- No register more than twice in any seven pieces. No template more than twice in any seven.
- The two rare sub-architectures (Statistic-to-Scene as a full spine, Satiric Ventriloquism) appear at most once a fortnight.
- Periodic-Build (9) and Withheld-Turn (8) both end on a held or refused beat. Do not run them on consecutive days; the back-to-back delayed-payoff rhythm is itself a sameness tell.
- Cap the terse one-line button close at one piece per batch (Samuel flagged it on 2026-06-04 as over-used).

## Per-piece pre-flight checklist

Run this on every piece before it joins the batch. Four gates, in order. A piece that fails an early gate goes back before the later gates are worth running. The first three gates are per-piece; the batch-sameness gate runs once over the whole week. Each gate points to its authority file.

### Gate 1: Accuracy and expression (per [[Accuracy-Guardrail]])

This is the gate that overrides craft. A beautifully written piece with a wrong fact is a failure, worse than a plain piece that gets everything right.
- Every name: spelled right, the right person or entity, correct title, traced to a source.
- Every number, figure, unit, date, and time period: re-read off the source it came from, not from memory or the draft.
- Every quote: verbatim, in quotation marks, correctly attributed, not pulled from a context that flips its meaning, checked against a primary or transcript source.
- Every causal claim (because, led to, drove, as a result): supported by a source, not inferred for narrative neatness. Correlation stated as causation is a failure.
- No orphan facts: nothing in the prose that is not on the numbered fact list and sourced. A vivid detail that appeared during drafting gets cut or sourced.
- Expression firewall: no sentence tracks a source's structure, follows its run of ideas in the same order, or reuses its distinctive phrasing. The test: could you have written this sentence knowing only the facts, without the source in front of you? If not, rewrite it.
- Final fidelity pass: read the piece once asking only "is each claim true and traceable", ignoring how it reads.

### Gate 2: Register-band check (per [[Register-Specs]])

Confirm the piece is actually in the register it was assigned, not the default smart-explainer voice.
- Sentence-length average sits inside this register's band (see the brief above and the full bands in [[Register-Specs]]).
- The min and max are present: at least one short sentence and at least one long one as the register requires.
- Burstiness matches the register (calmer for The Ledger, high for the hotter and more intimate registers). No three same-length sentences in a row.
- Opener variety matches the register; the piece does not open nearly every sentence on its grammatical subject.
- The register's signature device is present and load-bearing (parallelism for The Reckoning, antithesis and restraint for The Tribute, the short-against-long snap for The Long Look, the confiding refrain for The Witness Stand, the withheld turn for The Long Think, the periodic suspension for The Open Letter, attribution density for The Ledger).
- The ethos posture is right: the self is absent where it should be (Ledger, Long Look), present-as-reasoner (Long Think), present-as-witness (Witness Stand), receded behind the subject (Tribute).
- Drifted-if cue: read the opening cold. Could it be attributed to this register's voice specifically, or does it read like the generic calm explainer? If the latter, it has flattened.

### Gate 3: Anti-tell scan (per [[Anti-Tell-List]])

The fast mechanical pass. Run it every time, before any judgment call about quality.
1. Search for em-dashes and emojis. Any hit is an automatic fail. Remove and re-scan. (The Tribute and The Witness Stand are the highest-risk registers for an accidental dash; scan them twice.)
2. Search the Family A through F words and the significance-inflation phrases. Each hit: justify against the register or cut it.
3. Search sentence heads for "Additionally / Moreover / Furthermore / Notably / Importantly / Crucially / Indeed / Ultimately". More than one: rework.
4. Find every sentence that asserts something is important, significant, vital, a testament, a turning point, or a reminder. Replace with the concrete fact, or cut the beat. Highest-yield step.
5. Find every "serves as / stands as / represents / marks / features / boasts" and test whether "is" or "has" is the honest verb. If yes, use it.
6. Check burstiness: at least one sentence under 8 words and at least one over 30 (subject to the register's own band); no three same-length sentences in a row.
7. Check every factual claim names a real checkable source. Unattributed claim: cut it. This is both the AI-tell gate and the accuracy gate.
8. Check the close: it lands on a turn, button, or concrete stake, not a recap or a forward-looking homily.
9. Check the banned constructions: no "not just X but Y", no "it's not about X, it's about Y", no autopilot tricolon, no strained aphoristic wrap-up.
A piece that fails steps 1, 4, or 7 does not get a quality read. It goes back first.

### Gate 4: Batch-sameness check (per [[Cross-Piece-Sameness-Rubric]])

Run once over the whole week before any of it ships. The per-piece gates cannot see this; only the side-by-side pass can.
- Opener tally: read only the first sentence of every piece back to back. No opener bucket used more than twice.
- Closer tally: read only the last sentence of every piece. No closer bucket more than twice; the terse button capped at one.
- Paragraph-count column: counts spread across at least three values, not the same number four-plus times.
- Spine line per piece: no single arc shape shared by four or more pieces.
- Pet-phrase and first-word checklist: no glue word in three or more pieces; no connective opening a paragraph in three or more pieces.
- Blind-shuffle test (the highest-value check): strip topics and titles, read the openings cold. Do they read as five to seven different writers, or one writer on five to seven days? If you cannot tell them apart by voice, the palette has collapsed and you regenerate with the briefs above, not patch.
- Batch read-aloud: read all pieces aloud in one sitting. Deja vu by piece three means hunt the convergent rhythm or the register collapse.
- If any single rubric category trips, the batch does not ship until the offending pieces are reworked. Two or more tripped means the plan, not the pieces, is producing sameness: adjust the Variety Matrix and regenerate.
