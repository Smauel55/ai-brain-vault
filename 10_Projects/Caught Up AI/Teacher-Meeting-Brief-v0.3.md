# Teacher Meeting Brief v0.3

> Private run-of-show for the meeting with Samuel's own AP Lang teacher (target: ~2026-06-08). This is for Samuel, not a handover document. It supersedes the v0.2 in-room brief, which predates the AI-generation pivot, the "Opener" naming, and the real sample outputs. Disclosure stance for this meeting: open about AI from the start.

## Why this is a fresh brief, not an edit of v0.2

The product the v0.2 brief described is no longer the product. What changed:

- **Content engine flipped.** v0.2 assumed real articles sourced from open/CC publications with attribution (the "in production it would be a sourced piece" disclosure on the sample). That path is dead: licensing verbatim-plus-markup does not work. The pieces are now **written by AI from verified facts**, not reproduced from anyone's publication.
- **Naming.** The daily unit is an **"Opener"** (teacher copy + student copy per edition), not a "brief."
- **You have real artifacts now.** v0.2 carried one hand-written placeholder essay ("The Quiet Classroom"). You now have real, current-events sample Openers from the v2 prose engine across multiple registers. You can show finished product, not a mockup.
- **The central unknown moved.** v0.2's risk was sourcing/licensing. The risk now is **quality, authenticity, and accuracy**: can AI-written pieces pass an expert AP eye, and does AI authorship matter to a teacher? That is the spine of this meeting.
- **Cadence anxiety is gone.** The old "can we deliver 5 days a week" worry was a licensing-supply problem. AI generation removes it. The only remaining cadence cost is per-piece fact verification. So a cadence question now measures *demand* (how many days you'd use it), not supply.

## What I want out of this meeting (the four goals)

1. **Expert quality review.** Have her read real Openers and judge the prose, the device labels, the MCQs, the answer keys, the sample responses, and the AP alignment as an AP Lang expert. This is the validation Samuel cannot do himself.
2. **Authenticity verdict.** Find out whether the pieces read as machine-written, and whether that even matters to her as a teacher (open framing, since she will know they are AI).
3. **Discovery on the pain.** Confirm the prep-time pain, her real workflow, what she pays for, what makes her cancel.
4. **Recruit her.** Fall pilot participant, ongoing advisor, and/or judge on the human AP-Lang panel. Plus referrals.

> "I'm Samuel Levy, headed to Northwestern this fall, building this on my own. I'm not an AP Lang teacher and I've never run a classroom, which is exactly why I want your read.
>
> Caught Up AI is a daily ten-minute lesson opener for AP Lang: a short current-events nonfiction piece plus the full teaching layer, a student copy and a teacher copy, in your inbox by 6 a.m. One four-minute setup form, then nothing else to do.
>
> The thing I want to be straight about up front: the pieces are written by AI from verified facts, not pulled from real publications. I went down the licensing road and it does not work for a product that marks up the text. So the real question I need your help with is whether AI-written pieces are good enough, and honest enough, to put in front of AP students. I would rather you tell me where it breaks than be polite."

Then hand her a teacher-copy Opener and let her read before you say anything else.

## Block 1 — Discovery: workflow, pain, spend

1. Walk me through how you build a lesson opener or bell-ringer now. Where does the time actually go? *(real workflow, not the ideal one)*
2. When you use a current-events nonfiction piece, what do you still have to build yourself: the questions, the device markup, the answer key, the alignment? Which of those eats the most time? *(isolates the layer the product sells)*
3. What teaching tools do you pay for out of pocket? Have you cancelled one in the past year, and what made you cancel: a factual error, a pedagogical miss, unreliability, something else? *(real spend + the error category that sets the QA bar)*

## Block 2 — The artifact + expert review (highest value)

Hand her a **teacher-copy** Opener (lead with the Ledger; have a second register ready). Let her read first.

4. First reaction. Would you put this in front of students tomorrow? What is wrong with it? *(unprompted, before you steer)*
5. Is the length and reading level right for AP Lang? Is the register variety across days useful, or noise? *(format fit)*

**Expert-validation checklist (the surgical part, only an AP expert can do this).** Walk the teacher copy with her and get a yes/no plus a fix on each:

- **Device labels:** Is every marked device named correctly and actually present? Any mislabels (the classic one: anaphora vs parallelism)? Anything obvious that is unmarked?
- **MCQs:** Is there one defensibly-best answer per item? Does each distractor fail for a real reason, or can a student eliminate by feel? Are these AP-exam-valid in form?
- **Answer key reasoning:** Does the explanation hold up, or would it lose an argument with a sharp student?
- **Sample strong responses:** Would these actually earn the points?
- **Common misconceptions:** Are these the real ones students hit, or invented?
- **AP exam alignment:** Is the Q1/Q2/Q3 framing accurate? Is this piece better for rhetorical analysis, argument, or synthesis?

*(2+ expert objections on any single layer = that layer needs rework before pilot. Capture exact fixes in her words.)*

## Block 3 — AI acceptance, authenticity, accuracy

6. **Authenticity, open framing:** You know these are AI-written. Reading it, would you have known? Does it read as machine-written anywhere, and where? *(she is primed, so this is a ceiling not a clean blind read; the real blind read is the panel. Optional friendly test: show 2 pieces, ask which feels more human, even though she knows AI is in the mix.)*
7. Does it bother you that the piece is AI-written? Would it bother your students, your department, or your admin? *(the trust/disclosure question)*
8. **The sharp one (pedagogy):** AP rhetorical analysis asks students to analyze the choices a real writer made for a real audience and purpose. An AI piece has no real author and no real occasion. Does analyzing an AI's "choices" still teach the skill, or does it undercut it? Would you use these more for argument and reading practice than for pure rhetorical analysis? *(this is the deepest risk to the whole concept; let her talk.)*
9. How much would a single factual error cost you in trust? What is your tolerance: one error and you are out, or is a correction fine? *(sets the accuracy bar; we verify facts per piece, so this prices the verification labor.)*

## Block 4 — Pricing / willingness

10. If this landed ready-to-teach in your inbox every morning, what is it worth per month? Would you pay out of pocket, or does it have to go through the school? *(let her name a number before you do)*
11. Reaction to $19.99/month solo, $190/year? Too high, too low, about right? *(only after she names her own number)*
12. After a month of using it, what would make you cancel? *(the real churn trigger)*

## Block 5 — The ask: pilot, panel, referrals

13. Would you be a fall pilot: use real Openers in class for about four weeks and give me honest feedback? *(the primary ask)*
14. Would you sit on a small panel of AP Lang teachers doing a blind read, to settle whether the AI pieces pass? *(this is the decisive validation instrument; she is an ideal recruit and can suggest other judges.)*
15. Who else should I talk to: AP Lang teachers or AP coordinators whose read on teaching tools you trust? *(referrals, and a signal you earned her respect.)*

## What to bring / show

- 2 to 3 finished **teacher-copy** Openers across registers (lead with the **Ledger** as the daily default; bring one hotter register, e.g. Tribute or Reckoning, to show range). Current samples live in `10_Projects/Caught Up AI/Sample-Briefs/` (Ukraine Aid Vote / Ledger, Marjane Satrapi / Tribute, Antarctic Basin / Long Look, What the Waiting Did / Long Think). **Confirm the exact files the morning of; a parallel work session is building test Openers and filenames may change.**
- The matching **student copies**, so she sees both sides of an edition.
- Printed, not just on screen. Teachers redline on paper.
- Optional: the locked product spec, only if she asks for depth.

## The sharpest risk to walk in knowing

The AI-author pedagogy question (Block 3, Q8) is the one that can sink the concept, and an AP expert will have a real opinion. Do not argue it. If she says AI authorship undercuts rhetorical analysis, that is signal, not failure: it may push the product toward argument (Q3) and reading-skill practice rather than authorial-intent analysis (Q2), and toward transparent "practice text" framing. Let her draw the line.

## What got cut from v0.2 and why

- **The "sourced from open/CC publications with attribution" disclosure** on the sample: obsolete, the product no longer reproduces anyone's article.
- **"Idea stage, not built yet" framing:** you now have a working engine and real Openers; you are pre-launch, not pre-artifact.
- **The freshness-window probe** ("how recent does an article need to be"): moot under AI generation; recency is no longer supply-constrained.
- **The "would you want the article chosen for you too" shared-article question:** the licensing logic behind it is gone.
- **The hand-written "Quiet Classroom" placeholder:** replaced by real current-events Openers.
- **Already cut in v0.2 and staying cut:** hypothetical pain-magnitude scale, topics-to-avoid forced choice.
