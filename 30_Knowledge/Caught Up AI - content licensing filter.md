# Caught Up AI - content licensing filter

The rule for what published material Caught Up AI can legally use, given that the product (a) reproduces the full article text, (b) annotates/marks it up inline in the teacher version, and (c) sells access by paid subscription.

## The rule

A source is usable ONLY if its license permits all three at once: full reproduction, derivatives/modification, and commercial use. That means Public Domain, CC0, or CC-BY.
- CC-BY-SA technically qualifies but its ShareAlike clause forces the marked-up version to be released under CC-BY-SA too (recipients could redistribute it free). Treat as OFF for a paid product.
- ALL NonCommercial (NC) licenses are out: the product charges money.
- ALL NoDerivatives (ND) licenses are out: inline rhetorical markup is a derivative, and distributing it is barred.

Verified against Creative Commons' own legal codes via deep research, 2026-06-04, high confidence.

## Why ND is the silent killer

Inserting rhetorical-device highlighting and bracketed labels is "creating a derivative" under both Creative Commons and US copyright law (annotation is explicitly a derivative). ND permits only mechanical changes (format, spelling). So any ND license blocks the core teacher-version mechanic, even though ND content is otherwise free to republish.

## Implication

The free CC-BY/public-domain pool of current, argument-driven, classroom-safe nonfiction is too thin to anchor a daily product. Structural reason: free publishers choose NC or ND, and opinion outlets especially favor ND. This forces the content-engine decision (license vs AI-with-editing vs commission vs less-than-daily).
