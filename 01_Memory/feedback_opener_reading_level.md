Every term, name, or concept a Caught Up AI Opener uses WITHOUT explaining it must be common knowledge to a typical high schooler. If a specialist term is load-bearing, define it in a plain-language clause on first use; if it is not load-bearing, cut it. Trigger example: the $35-cap Ledger opener referenced Medicare "Part D" and "Part B" with no gloss — most high schoolers (and Samuel) do not know what those mean.

**Why:** the audience is teenagers (AP Lang, but still high schoolers). A piece a student cannot read without a glossary fails the product.

**How to apply:** Test — would a typical 11th grader who does not follow the news know this term with no help? If yes, use it bare (president, Congress, tax, recession, CEO, lawsuit). If no, gloss it in one clause on first use ("the part of Medicare that pays for prescription drugs", not "Part D") or cut it. NOT common knowledge: Medicare Part B/D, cloture/filibuster mechanics, basis points, mens rea, habeas corpus, P/E ratio, quantitative easing, appropriations-vs-authorizations, acronyms and numbered program parts (Section 230, Title IX). This is NOT "dumb down the prose" — AP-Lang sentence craft and sophisticated vocabulary stay; this governs assumed background knowledge and domain jargon only (the reading-grade bands in Register-Specs govern syntax). Do not over-explain genuine common knowledge — that is condescending and an AI tell. Encoded as Gate 0 in 10_Projects/Caught Up AI/Editorial-Standards.md.
